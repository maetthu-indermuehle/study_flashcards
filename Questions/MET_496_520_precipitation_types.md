## MET-496
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** precipitation, drizzle, metar

In aviation weather reports, what does drizzle generally mean?

- A) Very small liquid water drops falling from low cloud or fog
- B) Large ice stones falling from cumulonimbus
- C) Dry snow grains only
- D) Rain that evaporates before reaching the ground

**Answer:** A - Very small liquid water drops falling from low cloud or fog.

**Explanation:** Drizzle is made of very small liquid droplets and is often associated with low stratus, fog, and stable air. It can reduce visibility more than a pilot expects because the droplets are numerous and spread through a shallow layer. In temperatures near or below freezing, drizzle becomes much more serious because freezing drizzle can produce rapid airframe icing.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Present Weather; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Precipitation

## MET-497
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** precipitation, drizzle, visibility

Why can drizzle be operationally important for VFR flight even when it is light?

- A) It can reduce visibility and is often associated with low ceilings
- B) It always improves visibility
- C) It occurs only above 18,000 ft
- D) It prevents cloud from forming

**Answer:** A - It can reduce visibility and is often associated with low ceilings.

**Explanation:** Light drizzle may not look dramatic on radar or from the ground, but it often comes from low stratus and can occur with mist or fog. That combination can leave a VFR pilot with poor forward visibility and little vertical space below cloud. It is especially important near terrain or in circuits where good visual reference is essential.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather and Visibility; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI

## MET-498
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types

**Tags:** precipitation, snow-grains, metar

What are snow grains?

- A) Very small, white, opaque ice particles, often falling from stratus or fog
- B) Large hailstones from thunderstorms
- C) Liquid drops that freeze on impact
- D) Rain that evaporates before reaching the ground

**Answer:** A - Very small, white, opaque ice particles, often falling from stratus or fog.

**Explanation:** Snow grains are a winter precipitation type associated with stable, low-level cloud rather than strong convective cloud. They are smaller and less showery than snow showers. For pilots, their presence can point to low cloud, cold air, reduced visibility, and possible winter surface contamination.

## MET-499
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** precipitation, ice-pellets, frontal-weather

Ice pellets usually suggest what kind of temperature structure?

- A) Snow melted in a warm layer aloft, then refroze before reaching the surface
- B) The entire atmosphere is above freezing
- C) The air is dry from cloud base to the surface
- D) A thunderstorm is always overhead

**Answer:** A - Snow melted in a warm layer aloft, then refroze before reaching the surface.

**Explanation:** Ice pellets often form when snow falls through a layer of above-freezing air and melts, then falls into a deep enough subfreezing layer to refreeze before reaching the ground. This matters because the same general temperature setup can be near freezing rain conditions. Ice pellets can warn a pilot that warm air aloft and cold air below are interacting, which is an icing-significant pattern.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Winter Precipitation and Freezing Precipitation; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide

## MET-500
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** precipitation, freezing-rain, icing

What makes freezing rain hazardous to aircraft?

- A) It is liquid precipitation that freezes on contact with aircraft surfaces
- B) It is dry snow that bounces off wings
- C) It occurs only when airframe icing is impossible
- D) It is harmless if reported as light

**Answer:** A - It is liquid precipitation that freezes on contact with aircraft surfaces.

**Explanation:** Freezing rain can create rapid, heavy, and smooth ice accumulation. The drops are liquid when they strike the aircraft, then freeze on contact. For a non-icing-equipped light aircraft, freezing rain should be treated as a no-go condition because the ice can accumulate faster than the aircraft can safely tolerate.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Freezing Rain; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Aircraft Icing

## MET-501
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types

**Tags:** precipitation, freezing-drizzle, metar

What does `FZDZ` mean in a METAR?

- A) Freezing drizzle
- B) Freezing dust
- C) Fog decreasing
- D) Forecast drizzle

**Answer:** A - Freezing drizzle.

**Explanation:** `FZ` indicates freezing precipitation and `DZ` indicates drizzle. Freezing drizzle can contain supercooled droplets that freeze on impact. It may occur in shallow cloud or low stratus and can be easy to underestimate because it does not look as dramatic as heavy rain or thunderstorms.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Present Weather; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Freezing Drizzle

## MET-503
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** hail, thunderstorms, cumulonimbus

Hail is most commonly associated with which cloud type?

- A) Cumulonimbus
- B) Thin cirrus
- C) Fair-weather cumulus only
- D) Stratus with no vertical development

**Answer:** A - Cumulonimbus.

**Explanation:** Hail requires strong updrafts to hold growing ice particles in a cloud long enough for them to become large. That is a thunderstorm process, so hail points to cumulonimbus and severe convective weather. For pilots, hail is a warning sign for severe turbulence, strong vertical currents, lightning, and possible structural damage.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Thunderstorms and Hail; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Thunderstorms

## MET-504
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** hail, radar, avoidance

Why should a pilot avoid areas where hail is reported, even if the aircraft is not directly under the darkest cloud?

- A) Hail can be thrown or fall outside the heaviest visible precipitation core
- B) Hail only falls in clear air far from storms
- C) Hail proves the storm is weakening and safe
- D) Hail reduces turbulence

**Answer:** A - Hail can be thrown or fall outside the heaviest visible precipitation core.

**Explanation:** Thunderstorm updrafts and winds can carry hail away from the most obvious precipitation shaft. Radar and visual appearance do not guarantee a safe edge. If hail is present, the storm is strong enough to be dangerous, and a PPL should give it wide clearance rather than trying to pass close to the cell.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Thunderstorm Hazards; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Thunderstorm Avoidance

## MET-505
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** virga, evaporation, downdrafts

What is virga?

- A) Precipitation that falls from cloud but evaporates before reaching the ground
- B) Hail embedded in a thunderstorm
- C) Fog forming over snow
- D) Ice on a parked aircraft

**Answer:** A - Precipitation that falls from cloud but evaporates before reaching the ground.

**Explanation:** Virga appears as streaks or shafts below cloud that fade before reaching the surface. Evaporation cools the air, making it denser and encouraging downdrafts. For pilots, virga can signal localized sinking air, turbulence, wind shear, and reduced visibility beneath cloud bases.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Virga and Downdrafts; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Hazards

## MET-506
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** virga, downdraft, scenario

You see streaks of precipitation falling from a cloud base but disappearing before the ground. The air below looks hazy and winds are gusty. What hazard should you expect?

**Answer:** Expect possible downdrafts, turbulence, and wind shear associated with virga.

**Explanation:** The disappearing precipitation is virga. As precipitation evaporates, it cools the air and can accelerate a downdraft. Near the ground, that downdraft can spread out as gusty, shifting wind. A light aircraft may experience sudden sink or airspeed changes, so extra caution is needed, especially during takeoff, landing, or low-level flight.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Virga, Downdrafts, and Microbursts; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wind Shear

## MET-508
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** showers, precipitation, stability

What does showery precipitation usually suggest?

- A) Convective or unstable air with changing intensity
- B) Perfectly stable air with no vertical motion
- C) No turbulence risk
- D) Clear skies above

**Answer:** A - Convective or unstable air with changing intensity.

**Explanation:** Showers are usually produced by convective clouds, where rising currents create localized precipitation. They often start and stop abruptly and can vary strongly over short distances. For pilots, showery precipitation can mean bumps, reduced visibility in shafts, lower cloud bases nearby, and possible stronger convective development.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Stability and Showery Precipitation; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Air Masses and Stability

## MET-509
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types

Continuous precipitation over a wide area is most often associated with:

- A) Layered cloud and stable lifting, such as near a warm front
- B) Isolated fair-weather cumulus only
- C) Completely dry air
- D) Mountain waves only

**Answer:** A - Layered cloud and stable lifting, such as near a warm front.

**Explanation:** Widespread continuous rain or snow often forms when air is lifted gradually over a broad area. Warm fronts commonly produce this kind of layered cloud and precipitation. Operationally, it may bring long periods of low ceilings, reduced visibility, icing in cold layers, and fewer easy VFR gaps than a brief isolated shower.

## MET-512

**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** ice-pellets, freezing-rain, scenario

The METAR changes from ice pellets to freezing rain as the temperature remains below 0 C. What does this trend suggest?

**Answer:** It suggests an increasing freezing precipitation hazard, likely with liquid drops reaching the surface and freezing on contact.

**Explanation:** Ice pellets mean the drops had enough cold air below to refreeze before reaching the surface. A change to freezing rain can mean the cold surface layer is shallower or the warm layer aloft is more influential, allowing liquid drops to reach the ground while still supercooled. For aircraft, this is a serious icing signal and should trigger avoidance.

In METAR present weather, what does the prefix `-` usually indicate?

- A) Light intensity
- B) Severe turbulence
- C) Freezing precipitation

**Answer:** A - Light intensity.

- B) The air is perfectly stable with no vertical currents
- C) There is no cloud nearby

## MET-516
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** precipitation, interpretation, learning

Explain how precipitation type can help a pilot infer the weather structure aloft.

**Answer:**
Precipitation type gives clues about temperature, moisture, and vertical motion. Rain suggests liquid drops reaching the surface. Snow suggests the column is cold enough for ice crystals to survive. Ice pellets suggest melting aloft followed by refreezing below. Freezing rain or freezing drizzle suggests liquid drops are reaching a below-freezing surface layer. Hail suggests strong convective updrafts. Virga suggests dry air below cloud and possible downdrafts from evaporative cooling.

**Explanation:** A pilot should not treat precipitation codes as simple labels. They are evidence about the atmosphere between cloud and ground. That evidence helps identify icing risk, visibility reduction, turbulence, frontal structure, and whether the weather is stable and widespread or convective and localized.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Precipitation Processes; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Theory

## MET-517

**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** hail, thunderstorm, decision

ATC reports hail on the field 20 minutes before your planned arrival. The storm cell appears to be moving away, but lightning is still visible nearby. What should you do?

**Answer:** Delay or divert until the thunderstorm hazard has clearly moved away and conditions are safe.

**Explanation:** Hail means the storm has been strong. Even if it appears to be moving away, nearby lightning and recent hail suggest severe convective hazards may still be present: turbulence, gust fronts, wind shear, heavy precipitation, and hail outside the main core. A PPL should not rush an arrival just because the runway is technically visible.

Which precipitation type can reduce visibility without necessarily producing large radar returns?

- A) Drizzle
- B) Clear blue sky

## MET-519
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** virga, microburst, hot-dry

Why is virga often a warning sign in hot, dry conditions?

- A) Evaporating precipitation can create strong downdrafts and gusty outflow
- B) It proves no vertical motion exists
- C) It always means calm winds at the surface
- D) It removes turbulence risk

**Answer:** A - Evaporating precipitation can create strong downdrafts and gusty outflow.

**Explanation:** In dry air, falling precipitation can evaporate rapidly. Evaporation cools the air and makes it denser, which can accelerate downward. The resulting downdraft may produce gusty winds, blowing dust, turbulence, and wind shear even where little or no rain reaches the ground.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Virga and Microbursts; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wind Shear

## MET-520
**Topic:** Meteorology - Precipitation Types  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** integrated-decision, freezing-precipitation, vfr

Your route forecast includes rain changing to ice pellets, then freezing rain, with surface temperatures falling below 0 C near your ETA. What is the best VFR decision?

**Answer:** Do not plan to fly that route during the changeover; delay, cancel, or choose a route that avoids the freezing precipitation area.

**Explanation:** The sequence shows a developing winter precipitation hazard with warm air aloft and cold air near the surface. Ice pellets and freezing rain are strong clues that serious icing is possible. A non-icing-equipped light aircraft should not be in the air while the atmosphere is transitioning into freezing precipitation. The safest choice is to avoid the time and place of the hazard.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Winter Precipitation and Icing; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Aircraft Icing and Weather Decisions
