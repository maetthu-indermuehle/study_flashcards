## MET-571
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** clouds, families, low-clouds

Which cloud family is closest to the surface?

- A) Low clouds
- B) High clouds only
- C) Stratospheric ozone clouds
- D) Space clouds

**Answer:** A - Low clouds.

**Explanation:** Cloud families are commonly grouped by height: low, middle, high, and vertically developed. Low clouds are most important to VFR pilots because they directly affect ceilings, terrain clearance, circuit operations, and the ability to remain clear of cloud. Stratus, stratocumulus, and nimbostratus are common low-cloud examples.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Cloud Types; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds

## MET-572
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** clouds, low-clouds, stratus

Which cloud is most associated with a uniform low layer and possible drizzle?

- A) Stratus
- B) Cirrus
- C) Lenticular only
- D) Fair-weather cumulus only

**Answer:** A - Stratus.

**Explanation:** Stratus is a low, layered cloud. It often forms in stable air and may produce drizzle, mist, or low ceilings. For VFR pilots, stratus is important because it can cover a wide area and leave little vertical room below cloud, especially near terrain.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Stratus Clouds; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds and Stability

## MET-573

**Difficulty:** Basic

Which prefix is commonly used for middle-level clouds?

- A) Alto-
- B) Cirro-
- C) Nimbo- only
- D) Strato- only

**Answer:** A - Alto-.

**Explanation:** Middle clouds commonly use the prefix alto, as in altostratus and altocumulus. These clouds may not directly create a low ceiling, but they can signal approaching frontal weather, precipitation development, or instability aloft. A pilot should connect middle cloud trends with forecasts and pressure systems.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Cloud Classification; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds

## MET-574

Which prefix is commonly used for high clouds?

- A) Cirro-

## MET-575
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** clouds, vertical-development, cumulonimbus

Which cloud type has the greatest vertical development and thunderstorm hazard?

- A) Cumulonimbus
- B) Thin cirrus
- C) Stratus
- D) Altostratus only

**Answer:** A - Cumulonimbus.

**Explanation:** Cumulonimbus clouds are thunderstorm clouds. They can extend through a large depth of the atmosphere and contain severe turbulence, lightning, hail, heavy rain, icing, and strong updrafts and downdrafts. A PPL should avoid cumulonimbus by a wide margin rather than trying to fly near or under the core.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Cumulonimbus and Thunderstorms; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Thunderstorms

## MET-576
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** ceiling, cloud-base, metar

In aviation weather, what is a ceiling?

- A) The height of the lowest broken, overcast, or obscuring layer aloft
- B) The top of the highest cloud
- C) Any scattered cloud layer
- D) The height of the control tower

**Answer:** A - The height of the lowest broken, overcast, or obscuring layer aloft.

**Explanation:** A ceiling is not simply any cloud. Scattered cloud does not normally count as a ceiling, while broken or overcast layers do. Ceiling is central to VFR decisions because the pilot must maintain required cloud clearance and terrain clearance while keeping enough room for safe manoeuvring.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Sky Condition; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Minima

## MET-577
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** metar, broken, ceiling

Does `BKN020` normally count as a ceiling?

- A) Yes, a broken layer at 2,000 ft AGL is a ceiling
- B) No, broken cloud never counts
- C) Only if it is high cloud
- D) Only if the wind is calm

**Answer:** A - Yes, a broken layer at 2,000 ft AGL is a ceiling.

**Explanation:** In METAR coding, `BKN` means broken cloud coverage, and broken layers count as ceilings. The height is reported above ground level at the aerodrome. A 2,000 ft ceiling may be legal for some VFR operations but still marginal depending on terrain, route, visibility, and pilot experience.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Sky Condition; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, VFR Weather Decisions

## MET-578

Does `SCT018` normally count as a ceiling?

- A) No, scattered cloud does not normally count as a ceiling
- B) Yes, all cloud layers count as ceilings
- C) Only at night
- D) Only in winter

**Answer:** A - No, scattered cloud does not normally count as a ceiling.

**Explanation:** `SCT` means scattered cloud coverage. It may matter operationally, especially if cloud is building or terrain is nearby, but it is not normally a ceiling. A pilot should still avoid treating scattered cloud as irrelevant; scattered layers can become broken, hide terrain locally, or mark convective development.

## MET-579
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** cloud-bases, agl, metar

Cloud heights in METAR sky condition groups are reported:

- A) Above ground level at the reporting station
- B) Above sea level always
- C) As pressure altitude only
- D) In statute miles

**Answer:** A - Above ground level at the reporting station.

**Explanation:** METAR cloud bases are reported in hundreds of feet above the aerodrome elevation. This matters when comparing airport reports with enroute terrain. A 2,000 ft ceiling at a valley airport does not mean 2,000 ft clearance above nearby ridges. Pilots must convert the operational picture into terrain clearance along the route.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Sky Condition; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Briefing

## MET-580
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** ceiling, terrain, scenario

An airport METAR reports `BKN025`, but your route crosses terrain 1,800 ft above the airport elevation. Why may the route still be unsafe VFR?

**Answer:** The ceiling may leave only about 700 ft above the terrain before considering cloud clearance, rising ground, visibility, and escape options.

**Explanation:** METAR cloud bases are AGL at the airport, not above every point on the route. Terrain can rise into the cloud layer even when the airport looks VFR. A pilot must compare cloud bases to terrain elevation along the route, not just the departure field. This is especially important in hills and mountains.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Pilot Navigation - Weather and Terrain; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI

## MET-581
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** clouds, icing, supercooled-water

Which cloud condition can be a clue for structural icing?

- A) Visible moisture in cloud at temperatures near or below freezing
- B) Clear dry air far above cloud
- C) Warm dry air below the freezing level only
- D) Cloudless sky at high pressure

**Answer:** A - Visible moisture in cloud at temperatures near or below freezing.

**Explanation:** Structural icing needs moisture and a cold aircraft surface. Clouds made of supercooled liquid droplets are especially important. A cloud layer near or below freezing is not automatically icing everywhere, but it is a reason to check freezing levels, GFAs, AIRMETs, SIGMETs, and PIREPs before flying through or near it.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Aircraft Icing; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Aircraft Icing

## MET-582
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice

**Tags:** clouds, turbulence, cumulus

Which cloud clue suggests possible turbulence from vertical currents?

- A) Cumulus with strong vertical growth
- B) Uniform thin cirrostratus only
- C) Shallow radiation fog in calm air
- D) A clear sky at night over snow

**Answer:** A - Cumulus with strong vertical growth.

**Explanation:** Vertical development means air is rising strongly enough to build cloud upward. Where there are strong updrafts, there may also be downdrafts and turbulence. Towering cumulus and cumulonimbus are stronger warnings than small fair-weather cumulus.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Convective Clouds and Turbulence; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Stability

## MET-584
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** cumuliform, stratiform, stability

What is the broad difference between cumuliform and stratiform clouds?

- A) Cumuliform clouds are heaped and vertically developed; stratiform clouds are layered
- B) Cumuliform clouds are always high clouds only
- C) Stratiform clouds cannot produce precipitation
- D) There is no operational difference

**Answer:** A - Cumuliform clouds are heaped and vertically developed; stratiform clouds are layered.

**Explanation:** Cumuliform clouds usually point to unstable air and vertical motion, which can mean turbulence and showers. Stratiform clouds usually point to more stable lifting and can bring widespread low ceilings, drizzle, rain, snow, or icing. This broad visual distinction helps pilots connect cloud appearance to likely flight conditions.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Cloud Classification and Stability; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds

What operational warning can a cap cloud over a ridge provide?

- A) The ridge may be obscured by orographic cloud, with possible turbulence or icing
- B) The ridge is guaranteed clear below the cloud
- C) Winds are always calm
- D) No moisture is present

**Answer:** A - The ridge may be obscured by orographic cloud, with possible turbulence or icing.

**Explanation:** A cap cloud forms as moist air is lifted over terrain and condenses near the crest. It can hide the ridge and make a VFR crossing unsafe. If temperatures are cold enough, the same cloud can contain icing. If winds are strong, turbulence may also be present.

## MET-588
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds

**Tags:** cloud-tops, icing, escape

Why are cloud tops useful information when planning around icing?

- A) They help determine whether climbing above cloud is realistic
- B) They replace freezing level information
- C) They prove cloud contains no moisture
- D) They are used only for runway selection

**Answer:** A - They help determine whether climbing above cloud is realistic.

**Explanation:** If icing is possible in a cloud layer, one escape option may be climbing above it, but only if the tops are known and within aircraft capability. Unknown or rising tops can trap a pilot in icing conditions. Cloud tops should be considered with freezing level, aircraft performance, oxygen, terrain, and legal VFR cloud clearance.

## MET-591

Increasing cirrus followed by lowering middle and low cloud can be a sign of:

- A) An approaching warm front or layered weather system
- B) Guaranteed permanent clear weather
- C) No moisture in the atmosphere
- D) A local runway lighting failure

**Answer:** A - An approaching warm front or layered weather system.

**Explanation:** Warm fronts often show a cloud sequence as warm moist air overruns colder air: high cloud may arrive first, followed by middle cloud, then lower cloud and precipitation. Recognizing this trend helps a VFR pilot anticipate deteriorating ceilings and visibility before the airport report becomes marginal.

## MET-592
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** clouds, icing, turbulence, learning

Explain how cloud appearance can help a pilot anticipate icing or turbulence.

**Answer:**
Layered cloud in cold air can suggest widespread icing potential, especially if it contains supercooled liquid water. Cumulus with strong vertical growth suggests convective turbulence and possibly showers or thunderstorms. Lenticular or rotor clouds suggest mountain wave and turbulence. Cap clouds over terrain can mean obscuration, icing, or turbulence. Low stratus may signal low ceilings, drizzle, and poor visibility.

**Explanation:** Clouds are visible evidence of moisture and vertical motion. They do not replace official products, but they help a pilot interpret what the atmosphere is doing in real time. The best decisions combine visual cloud clues with METARs, TAFs, GFAs, radar, satellite, PIREPs, and terrain awareness.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Clouds and Weather Hazards; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds

## MET-594
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Multiple Choice

**Tags:** fog, cloud-base, surface

Fog can be thought of operationally as:

- A) Cloud at or near the surface
- B) High cirrus above 20,000 ft
- C) Dry dust only
- D) A wind report

**Answer:** A - Cloud at or near the surface.

**Explanation:** Fog is made of tiny water droplets or ice crystals suspended near the surface, reducing visibility. Operationally, it creates the same kind of visual problem as being in cloud, but at ground level. It can prevent VFR departure or arrival even when skies above are clear.

## MET-595
**Topic:** Meteorology - Clouds  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** integrated-decision, cloud-base, icing

You plan to fly VFR below a cloud layer. The ceiling is forecast 2,500 ft AGL, terrain rises along the route, and the freezing level is near 3,000 ft. What should you consider?

**Answer:** Consider whether there is enough legal and practical clearance below cloud and above terrain, and whether any climb into cloud would create icing risk.

**Explanation:** The plan depends on a narrow vertical space. Rising terrain reduces room below the ceiling, and the freezing level near cloud height means any accidental or required climb into cloud could involve icing. A safer plan may be to delay, reroute over lower terrain, or choose conditions with higher ceilings and better margins.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, VFR Weather Decisions and Aircraft Icing; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, GFA and METAR/TAF
