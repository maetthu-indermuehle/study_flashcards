## MET-546
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** turbulence, definition, flight-safety

What is turbulence?

- A) Irregular air motion that causes changes in aircraft attitude, altitude, or load
- B) A type of precipitation only
- C) A cloud height report
- D) A steady tailwind

**Answer:** A - Irregular air motion that causes changes in aircraft attitude, altitude, or load.

**Explanation:** Turbulence is caused by uneven air movement. It may come from convection, terrain, wind shear, fronts, jet streams, thunderstorms, or wake vortices. For a PPL, turbulence matters because it affects passenger comfort, aircraft control, structural loading, approach stability, and the ability to maintain altitude near terrain.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Turbulence; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Hazards

## MET-547
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** frontal-turbulence, fronts, wind-shear

Why can fronts produce turbulence?

- A) Air masses with different temperature, wind, and stability meet and create lift and wind shear
- B) Fronts remove all vertical motion
- C) Fronts occur only in calm winds
- D) Fronts eliminate clouds

**Answer:** A - Air masses with different temperature, wind, and stability meet and create lift and wind shear.

**Explanation:** A front is a boundary between air masses. Wind direction, wind speed, temperature, and vertical motion can change across that boundary. The resulting lift, shear, and precipitation can produce turbulence. Cold fronts are often more abrupt, while warm fronts may have broader areas of cloud, icing, and embedded turbulence.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Fronts and Turbulence; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Fronts

## MET-548
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** cold-front, turbulence, showers

Turbulence near a cold front is often strongest when:

- A) The front is fast-moving with strong wind shifts, showers, or thunderstorms
- B) The air is calm and cloudless everywhere
- C) The front has no temperature change
- D) The front is shown only on a map with no weather

**Answer:** A - The front is fast-moving with strong wind shifts, showers, or thunderstorms.

**Explanation:** Fast-moving cold fronts can lift warm air rapidly and produce sharp wind shifts, convective showers, squalls, or thunderstorms. These features create mechanical and convective turbulence. A pilot should pay attention to frontal speed, pressure changes, radar, METAR trends, TAFs, GFAs, and PIREPs.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Cold Fronts; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Frontal Weather

## MET-549
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** wake-turbulence, vortices, aircraft

What causes wake turbulence?

- A) Wingtip vortices generated by aircraft producing lift
- B) Ice pellets falling from cloud
- C) A low altimeter setting
- D) The magnetic compass turning error

**Answer:** A - Wingtip vortices generated by aircraft producing lift.

**Explanation:** An aircraft wing creates lift by producing pressure differences and downward deflection of air. This creates rotating wake vortices, strongest behind heavy, slow, clean aircraft. Wake turbulence can roll a following aircraft violently, especially near the ground where recovery altitude is limited.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wake Turbulence; FAA Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge, Wake Turbulence

## MET-550
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** wake-turbulence, wind, runway

How can a light crosswind affect wake turbulence on a runway?

- A) It can move one vortex away while keeping the other near or over the runway
- B) It always eliminates wake turbulence
- C) It makes vortices rise above traffic
- D) It prevents vortices from forming

**Answer:** A - It can move one vortex away while keeping the other near or over the runway.

**Explanation:** Wake vortices normally sink and drift with the wind. A light crosswind can push one vortex away and hold the other near the runway or a parallel runway. This is why pilots should consider wind direction and speed when deciding where to rotate, where to touch down, and how long to wait behind larger aircraft.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wake Turbulence Avoidance; FAA Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge, Wake Turbulence

## MET-551
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** wake-turbulence, weather-interaction, calm-wind

Why can calm or light-wind conditions be unfavorable for wake turbulence?

- A) Vortices may remain near the runway or flight path longer
- B) Vortices cannot exist in calm air
- C) Calm air guarantees wake will rise
- D) Calm wind removes landing risk

**Answer:** A - Vortices may remain near the runway or flight path longer.

**Explanation:** Stronger winds may move wake away or break it up more quickly, though they bring other hazards. In calm or light winds, wake vortices can linger near the runway environment. This is especially important for a light aircraft departing or landing behind a larger aircraft.

**Reference:** FAA Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge, Wake Turbulence; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wake Turbulence

## MET-552
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** wake-turbulence, takeoff, scenario

A heavy aircraft departs ahead of you from the same runway. Where should you plan to lift off?

**Answer:** Plan to lift off before the heavy aircraft's rotation point and remain above or upwind of its flight path if safe and practical.

**Explanation:** Wake vortices begin when the aircraft lifts off and then sink and drift. A light aircraft should avoid climbing through the heavy aircraft's wake. If you cannot safely lift off before the rotation point or maintain a safe path, wait for more spacing. The correct technique depends on runway length, wind, obstacles, and ATC instructions.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wake Turbulence Avoidance; FAA Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge, Wake Turbulence

## MET-553
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** wake-turbulence, landing, scenario

You are landing behind a larger aircraft on the same runway. Where should you aim to touch down?

**Answer:** Stay above the larger aircraft's approach path and touch down beyond its touchdown point, if runway length and conditions allow.

**Explanation:** Wake vortices are generated along the larger aircraft's flight path and sink below it. Staying above the path and touching down beyond the touchdown point helps avoid the strongest wake. If spacing, wind, runway length, or stability is not adequate, request additional spacing or go around.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wake Turbulence Avoidance; FAA Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge, Wake Turbulence

## MET-555
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** turbulence-reporting, moderate-turbulence, pirep

What does moderate turbulence generally imply?

- A) Changes in altitude or attitude occur, but the aircraft remains in positive control
- B) No noticeable aircraft movement
- C) Aircraft is always structurally damaged
- D) Wake turbulence only

**Answer:** A - Changes in altitude or attitude occur, but the aircraft remains in positive control.

**Explanation:** Moderate turbulence is more than light chop. Occupants feel definite strain against seat belts, unsecured objects may move, and maintaining altitude or attitude becomes more difficult. For a student or PPL in a light aircraft, forecast or reported moderate turbulence may exceed personal comfort or safety margins, especially near terrain.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, PIREP Turbulence Intensity; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Turbulence

## MET-560
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** turbulence, maneuvering-speed, aircraft-control

Why should a pilot slow to the recommended turbulence penetration speed in rough air?

- A) To reduce structural loads and avoid excessive airspeed excursions
- B) To increase structural loads
- C) To make wake turbulence stronger
- D) To remove the need for seat belts

**Answer:** A - To reduce structural loads and avoid excessive airspeed excursions.

**Explanation:** In turbulence, gusts can rapidly change angle of attack and load factor. Flying too fast can create high structural loads; flying too slow can increase stall risk. The aircraft POH provides guidance for rough-air or maneuvering speed. The pilot should avoid abrupt control inputs and keep the aircraft under control rather than chase altitude precisely.

**Reference:** Aircraft POH; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Turbulence and Aircraft Handling

## MET-561
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** mechanical-turbulence, wind, terrain

Where is mechanical turbulence most likely?

- A) Downwind of obstacles, rough terrain, ridges, buildings, or tree lines in strong wind
- B) In perfectly calm air only
- C) Above a smooth ocean with no wind
- D) Inside the altimeter

**Answer:** A - Downwind of obstacles, rough terrain, ridges, buildings, or tree lines in strong wind.

**Explanation:** Mechanical turbulence forms when wind flows over or around surface obstacles. The stronger the wind and the rougher the terrain, the stronger the turbulence can be. Near the ground, it can affect takeoff, landing, and low-level manoeuvring because there is little altitude to recover from sudden sink or roll.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Mechanical Turbulence; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Low-Level Turbulence

A PIREP along your route reports occasional moderate turbulence below 5,000 ft near a fast-moving cold front. You are planning a student solo cross-country. What should you consider?

**Answer:** Consider delaying, rerouting, or choosing an altitude and route away from the front; the report may exceed student solo personal minima.

A PIREP is actual weather, not just a forecast, so it deserves strong weight in the decision.

## MET-564

**Tags:** mountain-wave, turbulence, lenticular

What cloud may indicate mountain wave activity and possible severe turbulence?

- A) Lenticular cloud
- B) Fog only
- C) Thin uniform cirrus only
- D) Contrail only

**Answer:** A - Lenticular cloud.

**Explanation:** Lenticular clouds form in standing waves downwind of mountains when moist air rises and cools in the wave. Smooth-looking lenticular clouds can be misleading because severe turbulence may exist nearby, especially in rotor zones below the wave. Light aircraft should treat mountain wave clues with caution.

## MET-567
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** turbulence, rough-air-speed, scenario

You enter unexpected moderate turbulence in cruise. What aircraft handling response is generally appropriate?

**Answer:** Slow to the aircraft's recommended turbulence or maneuvering speed, avoid abrupt control inputs, secure the cockpit, and consider changing altitude or route.

**Explanation:** The goal is to reduce structural loading while maintaining control. Chasing altitude aggressively can create unnecessary control inputs and loads. If turbulence is associated with weather, terrain, or a front, the better long-term answer may be to leave the area rather than simply endure it.

## MET-568
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** pirep, turbulence-reporting, learning

What information should be included in a useful turbulence PIREP?

**Answer:**
A useful turbulence PIREP should include location, time, altitude, aircraft type, turbulence intensity, frequency, duration or layer, and any related weather such as cloud, mountain wave, wind shear, or icing. If possible, include whether the turbulence was in cloud, below cloud, near terrain, near a front, or in clear air.

**Explanation:** Turbulence affects aircraft differently, so aircraft type and altitude matter. A report of moderate turbulence from a large aircraft may be more serious for a small trainer. The more precise the report, the more useful it becomes for briefers, forecasters, and other pilots choosing routes and altitudes.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, PIREP; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Turbulence Reports

## MET-569
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** turbulence, cloud-clues, cumulus

Which cloud clue often suggests convective turbulence?

- A) Towering cumulus with strong vertical development
- B) Uniform thin cirrus only
- C) Clear sky with no heating
- D) Radiation fog in calm wind only

**Answer:** A - Towering cumulus with strong vertical development.

**Explanation:** Vertical cloud growth means rising air is strong enough to build the cloud upward. The same rising and sinking currents that form the cloud can produce turbulence. If the cloud grows into cumulonimbus, hazards increase further to include lightning, hail, heavy rain, wind shear, and severe turbulence.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Convective Clouds and Turbulence; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds

## MET-570
**Topic:** Meteorology - Turbulence  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** integrated-decision, wake-turbulence, weather

You are number two behind a large aircraft on a calm evening. A light quartering tailwind exists on final. What wake turbulence issue should you consider?

**Answer:** Wake vortices may linger and drift toward your approach path; request spacing, stay above the preceding path, and be prepared to go around.

**Explanation:** Calm or light wind can allow wake to persist, and a quartering tailwind can move vortices into unexpected positions on final. A light aircraft close behind a larger one has little margin near the ground. Wake avoidance requires spacing, path management, and willingness to reject the approach if the situation is not clearly safe.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Wake Turbulence; FAA Pilot's Handbook of Aeronautical Knowledge, Wake Turbulence
