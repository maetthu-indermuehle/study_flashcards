## MET-396
**Topic:** Meteorology - Mountain Operational Weather  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** mountain-weather, operational-risk, vfr

Why does mountain flying require extra weather margin compared with flat terrain?

- A) Terrain reduces escape options and magnifies wind, cloud, visibility, and performance hazards
- B) Weather is always calmer in mountains
- C) Legal weather minima do not apply in mountains
- D) Clouds cannot form near mountains

**Answer:** A - Terrain reduces escape options and magnifies wind, cloud, visibility, and performance hazards.

**Explanation:** Mountains add several risks at once: rising terrain, fewer emergency landing areas, narrow valleys, changing winds, mechanical turbulence, downdrafts, rapid cloud formation, and route bottlenecks such as passes. A ceiling or visibility that might be comfortable over flat farmland can be unsafe in a valley or near a ridge because the pilot has less room to turn, climb, descend, or divert. Mountain weather decisions should therefore use more conservative personal minima.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Pilot Navigation - Weather and Choosing an Altitude; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 16, Mountain Weather

## MET-397
**Topic:** Meteorology - Mountain Operational Weather  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** mountain-obscuration, definition, vfr

What is mountain obscuration?

- A) Mountains or ridges hidden by cloud, precipitation, smoke, fog, or other visibility restrictions
- B) A type of altimeter error only
- C) A chart symbol for high pressure
- D) Snow on a runway

**Answer:** A - Mountains or ridges hidden by cloud, precipitation, smoke, fog, or other visibility restrictions.

**Explanation:** Mountain obscuration means the terrain itself is not reliably visible. For a VFR pilot, this is a serious hazard because mountain flying depends on seeing terrain, passes, ridgelines, valley exits, and escape routes. The aircraft may technically be outside cloud while the terrain ahead is still hidden. That can lead to controlled flight into terrain if the pilot continues into an obscured valley or pass.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Mountain Obscuration; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, GFA and AIRMET/SIGMET Weather Products

## MET-398

Which conditions can cause mountain obscuration?

- A) Clouds, precipitation, fog, smoke, haze, blowing snow, or other obscurations
- B) Only thunderstorms
- C) Only clear air turbulence
- D) Only incorrect altimeter settings

**Answer:** A - Clouds, precipitation, fog, smoke, haze, blowing snow, or other obscurations.

**Explanation:** Mountain obscuration is not limited to cloud. Smoke from wildfire, blowing snow, fog, rain, snow showers, and low stratus can all hide terrain. This matters because a pilot might see acceptable airport weather nearby but still have a mountain route blocked by obscuration. Terrain can disappear gradually as contrast decreases, especially in snow, smoke, or flat light.

## MET-399
**Topic:** Meteorology - Mountain Operational Weather  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** mountain-obscuration, scenario, turnback

You are approaching a pass VFR. The valley behind you is clear, but the ridge line ahead is disappearing into cloud and snow showers. What should you do?

**Answer:** Turn back or divert early while the valley behind remains clear and there is room to manoeuvre.

**Explanation:** This is a classic mountain obscuration trap. The fact that the aircraft is still in VMC at the moment does not make the route safe if the pass and ridge are disappearing. Continuing may lead to a narrowing valley, rising terrain, and no safe turning room. The best mountain weather decision is usually made before the aircraft is forced to make one.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Considerations - Encountering Weather Below VFR Conditions; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Mountain Obscuration

**Topic:** Meteorology - Mountain Operational Weather

Why does fog commonly form in mountain valleys overnight?

- A) Cold dense air drains into valleys, cools the valley air to saturation, and traps moisture
- B) Valleys always have stronger sunlight at night
- C) Mountains prevent all condensation
- D) Jet streams descend into valleys

**Answer:** A - Cold dense air drains into valleys, cools the valley air to saturation, and traps moisture.

**Explanation:** At night, slopes cool and dense air drains downhill into valleys. This cold air can pool in low areas, lowering temperature toward dew point. If moisture is present and wind is light, valley fog can form. It may be very localized: hilltops or ridges can be clear while the valley floor and airport are fogged in. This is important for both departure planning and diversion planning.

Why can valley fog be slow to dissipate?

- A) Cold air and moisture can remain trapped in the valley, especially with weak sun or an inversion
- B) Fog always dissipates instantly after sunrise
- C) Valleys cannot have inversions
- D) Fog is unaffected by terrain

**Answer:** A - Cold air and moisture can remain trapped in the valley, especially with weak sun or an inversion.

Cold air pools low, sunlight may reach the valley floor later, and surrounding terrain can block wind that would otherwise mix the fog out. A pilot should wait for actual improvement, not just predicted burnoff.

## MET-408
**Topic:** Meteorology - Mountain Operational Weather

**Tags:** mountain-waves, wind, turbulence

Mountain waves are most likely when:

- A) Strong stable wind blows across a mountain range
- B) Calm air sits over flat terrain
- C) Warm dry air remains motionless
- D) Fog forms over a lake

**Answer:** A - Strong stable wind blows across a mountain range.

**Explanation:** Mountain waves form when stable air is forced over terrain and then oscillates downstream. They can extend far above and downwind of the mountains. For pilots, mountain waves are important because they can produce strong updrafts, downdrafts, severe turbulence, rotor zones, and rapid altitude or airspeed changes. They can occur even when the sky is clear.

What do lenticular clouds often indicate?

- A) Mountain wave activity
- B) Radiation fog only
- C) Calm air at all altitudes
- D) Sea breeze

**Answer:** A - Mountain wave activity.

**Explanation:** Lenticular clouds are smooth, lens-shaped clouds that can form in standing waves downwind of terrain. They are visual evidence that strong wave motion exists in the atmosphere. The cloud itself may look smooth, but the surrounding air can include strong vertical currents and turbulence, especially near rotor zones below the wave crests.

Why are rotor zones hazardous?

- A) They can contain severe rolling turbulence and strong vertical currents below mountain waves
- B) They always mark smooth air
- C) They occur only over oceans
- D) They are a type of runway marking

**Answer:** A - They can contain severe rolling turbulence and strong vertical currents below mountain waves.

**Explanation:** A rotor is a turbulent horizontal circulation that may form below a mountain wave crest on the lee side of a ridge. Rotor clouds can look like ragged cumulus, but the air around them may be violent. Light aircraft can experience sudden altitude changes, rolling moments, and loss of control risk. Rotor zones should be avoided, not inspected closely.
