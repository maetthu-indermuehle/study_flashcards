## Q001
**Topic:** Air Law — Collision Avoidance
**Type:** Multiple Choice

When two aircraft are converging at approximately the same altitude, which aircraft must give way?

- A) The aircraft on the left
- B) The aircraft that has the other on its right
- C) The faster aircraft
- D) The higher aircraft

**Answer:** B — The aircraft that has the other on its right

**Explanation:** Under CAR 602.19, when two aircraft are converging at approximately the same altitude, the aircraft that has the other on its right must give way. Put simply: the aircraft on the right has right of way. Options A, C, and D are not correct — speed and altitude are not factors in this rule.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19; TP 11919 PSTAR S.1

## Q002
**Topic:** Air Law — Collision Avoidance
**Type:** Multiple Choice

When two aircraft are approaching head-on (or approximately so) and there is a danger of collision, each pilot shall:

- A) Climb to avoid the other
- B) Alter heading to the left
- C) Alter heading to the right
- D) Slow down and let the other pass

**Answer:** C — Alter heading to the right

**Explanation:** The head-on rule requires both pilots to alter heading to the right so each aircraft passes to the right of the other. This mirrors road traffic rules. Options A and B are incorrect — the rule specifically says right, not left or climb.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19; TP 11919 PSTAR S.1

## Q003
**Topic:** Air Law — Collision Avoidance
**Type:** Multiple Choice

When overtaking another aircraft, the overtaking pilot shall:

- A) Pass above the other aircraft
- B) Alter heading to the left
- C) Alter heading to the right
- D) Sound three short horn blasts

**Answer:** C — Alter heading to the right

**Explanation:** An overtaking aircraft alters heading to the right so it passes to the right of the slower aircraft. The overtaken aircraft has right of way and need not alter course. Passing above or to the left is not the prescribed rule.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19; TP 11919 PSTAR S.1

## Q004
**Topic:** Air Law — Right of Way
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Which of the following has the highest right of way priority?

- A) A glider
- B) An aircraft towing a glider
- C) An airship
- D) An aircraft in distress

**Answer:** D — An aircraft in distress

**Explanation:** The right-of-way priority order from highest to lowest is: (1) aircraft in distress, (2) balloons, (3) gliders, (4) aircraft towing gliders or objects, (5) airships, (6) aeroplanes and helicopters, (7) lighter-than-air except balloons. An aircraft in distress always has absolute priority.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19; TP 11919 PSTAR S.1

## Q005
**Topic:** Air Law — Right of Way
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Which of the following has the LOWEST right-of-way priority among powered aircraft in flight?

- A) A balloon
- B) A glider
- C) An aircraft towing a banner
- D) A powered aeroplane not towing anything

**Answer:** D — A powered aeroplane not towing anything

**Explanation:** The priority order places balloons second (after distress), gliders third, and aircraft towing objects fourth — all above a standard powered aeroplane, which is sixth in the hierarchy. A powered aeroplane must give way to balloons, gliders, and towing aircraft.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19; TP 11919 PSTAR S.1

## Q006
**Topic:** Air Law — Right of Way
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Two aircraft are approaching to land at the same uncontrolled aerodrome. Aircraft A is on final at 800 ft AAE and Aircraft B is on final at 600 ft AAE. Which has right of way?

- A) Aircraft A, because it is higher and more established
- B) Aircraft B, because it is at the lower altitude
- C) The faster aircraft
- D) They must both go around

**Answer:** B — Aircraft B, because it is at the lower altitude

**Explanation:** When two aircraft are on final approach to land, the aircraft at the lower altitude has right of way. However, the lower aircraft may not cut in front of or overtake the higher aircraft on final. Aircraft B at 600 ft AAE has right of way over Aircraft A at 800 ft AAE.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19

## Q008
**Topic:** Air Law — Visual Signals
**Type:** Multiple Choice

A steady GREEN light signal from the control tower to an aircraft in flight means:

- A) Cleared to approach
- B) Cleared to land
- C) Give way and continue circling
- D) Return to starting point

**Answer:** B — Cleared to land

**Explanation:** Light gun signals from the tower to aircraft in flight: steady green = cleared to land; flashing green = cleared to approach; steady red = give way, continue circling; flashing red = aerodrome unsafe, do not land. A steady green is the final clearance to land.

**Reference:** CAR 602.96; TP 11919 PSTAR S.2

**Answer:** C — Cleared to approach (return and land)

## Q010

**Answer:**

## Q016

## Q026
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace
**Type:** Open Answer

Describe the seven classes of Canadian airspace, including altitude bands, type of operations permitted, and requirements for VFR entry.

**Answer:**
Class A: FL180 and above. IFR only — no VFR permitted. ATC clearance required for all aircraft.
Class B: 12,500 ft ASL to FL180. IFR and VFR permitted. Transponder with Mode C required. VFR requires ATC clearance.
Class C: Controlled terminal area. IFR and VFR permitted. VFR requires ATC clearance, two-way radio, and transponder Mode C. ATC provides IFR/IFR and IFR/VFR separation; VFR/VFR traffic info.
Class D: Control zones (surface to base of CZ). IFR and VFR permitted. VFR requires two-way radio communication established — no clearance needed. ATC provides IFR/IFR separation and traffic info for VFR.
Class E: Controlled airspace not classified A–D. IFR requires clearance; VFR no clearance or transponder required.
Class F: Special use airspace. Restricted (prohibited without permission) or Advisory (traffic information provided).
Class G: Uncontrolled airspace. No ATC clearance, transponder, or radio required for VFR.

**Explanation:** Understanding airspace classes is fundamental to flight planning. The primary differentiator is what ATC does for you in each class and what you must have to enter. Class D is often confused with Class C — the key difference is that Class D requires radio contact (not a clearance) and does not mandate a transponder.

**Reference:** CAR 601; TP 12880

## Q070
**Topic:** Air Law — Aerodrome Operations
**Type:** Open Answer

Explain the procedures for joining the circuit at an uncontrolled aerodrome with an ATF (Aerodrome Traffic Frequency).

**Answer:**

## Q074
**Topic:** Air Law — Emergency Procedures
**Type:** Open Answer

What are the correct procedures and format for declaring a MAYDAY distress call?

**Answer:**
Transmit on the distress frequency (121.5 MHz) or any available frequency. Speak clearly and slowly. The format is:
1. "MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY" (spoken three times)
2. The name of the ATS unit being called
3. "THIS IS [aircraft type and call sign, spoken three times]"
4. "MAYDAY [call sign]" then: nature of distress, position, altitude, heading, airspeed, intentions, number of persons on board, fuel endurance, and any other pertinent information.
Also: squawk 7700 on the transponder.
Example: "MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. Vancouver Centre, this is Cessna Golf Foxtrot Lima Mike, Golf Foxtrot Lima Mike, Golf Foxtrot Lima Mike. MAYDAY, Golf Foxtrot Lima Mike, engine failure, 30 miles northeast of Vancouver, 5,000 feet, heading 090, 90 knots, two persons on board, 1 hour fuel. Request immediate assistance."

**Explanation:** MAYDAY has absolute priority on any frequency — all other stations must cease transmissions. The repetition of "MAYDAY" three times and the call sign three times ensures the nature of the call is unmistakable even in poor radio conditions.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA VFR Phraseology Guide; TP 11919 PSTAR S.3

- B) Because the center of the retina (fovea) has few rods, so looking slightly off-center uses the more light-sensitive rods
- C) To increase peripheral vision of terrain
- D) To avoid flicker vertigo from instrument panels

**Answer:** B — Because the center of the retina (fovea) has few rods, so looking slightly off-center uses the more light-sensitive rods

**Explanation:** Rods (the cells used for low-light vision) are concentrated in the peripheral retina, not at the center (fovea). Looking directly at a dim object at night causes it to disappear because the fovea has very few rods. Shifting the gaze 10–15° off-center places the image on the rod-rich peripheral retina, making it visible.

**Reference:** TP 12880; TP 11919 PSTAR S.8

## Q100
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace
**Type:** Open Answer

Explain the ADIZ (Air Defence Identification Zone) and what a VFR pilot must do when operating within it.

**Answer:**
The Air Defence Identification Zone (ADIZ) is a defined airspace extending around Canada in which aircraft must comply with special identification procedures for national security. Within the ADIZ, pilots must: (1) File a flight plan (IFR or VFR) before entering; (2) Transmit a position report to the appropriate ATC unit within 5 minutes of crossing the ADIZ boundary or as instructed; (3) Have an operable transponder; (4) Maintain two-way radio communication capability. Failure to comply may result in military interception. The ADIZ boundaries are shown on aeronautical charts.

**Explanation:** The ADIZ requirements exist to prevent unauthorized intrusions into Canadian airspace. During pre-flight planning for any flight that might cross the ADIZ, pilots must review procedures in the TC AIM. Interception procedures should be understood by all pilots operating near ADIZ boundaries.

**Reference:** CAR 602.144; TC AIM RAC 2.8
