## MET-596
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** stability, definition, lapse-rate

What does atmospheric stability describe?

- A) Whether lifted air tends to keep rising or return toward its original level
- B) The strength of the aircraft structure
- C) The accuracy of the compass
- D) The runway surface condition

**Answer:** A - Whether lifted air tends to keep rising or return toward its original level.

**Explanation:** Stability describes the atmosphere's resistance to vertical motion. In stable air, a lifted parcel tends to sink back. In unstable air, a lifted parcel tends to keep rising. This idea explains many aviation weather differences: layered cloud versus cumulus, smooth air versus turbulence, and steady precipitation versus showers.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Atmospheric Stability; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Stability

## MET-597
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** stable-air, stratiform, smooth-air

Stable air is commonly associated with:

- A) Layered clouds, smooth air, haze, fog, or steady precipitation
- B) Towering cumulus and severe convection only
- C) No clouds ever
- D) Better visibility in haze

**Answer:** A - Layered clouds, smooth air, haze, fog, or steady precipitation.

**Explanation:** Stable air resists vertical mixing. Moist stable air often forms stratus-type clouds, fog, drizzle, or widespread precipitation. Because mixing is weak, haze and pollutants can remain trapped near the surface. Smooth air may sound pleasant, but stable conditions can still be poor for VFR if ceilings or visibility are low.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Stable Air Characteristics; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Stability

## MET-598
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** unstable-air, cumuliform, turbulence

Unstable air is commonly associated with:

- A) Cumuliform clouds, turbulence, showers, and good visibility outside precipitation
- B) Only fog and no vertical motion
- C) Smooth layered cloud only
- D) No wind at any altitude

**Answer:** A - Cumuliform clouds, turbulence, showers, and good visibility outside precipitation.

**Explanation:** Unstable air encourages vertical motion. Rising currents form cumulus clouds and can produce showers or thunderstorms if enough moisture and lift exist. The mixing can improve visibility between showers, but it also creates turbulence and rapidly changing local weather.

## MET-599
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** lapse-rate, stability, temperature

Why does the environmental lapse rate matter?

- A) It helps determine whether lifted air will be warmer or colder than its surroundings
- B) It gives runway heading
- C) It measures radio range
- D) It replaces weather reports

**Answer:** A - It helps determine whether lifted air will be warmer or colder than its surroundings.

**Explanation:** The environmental lapse rate is how temperature changes with height in the surrounding atmosphere. If a lifted air parcel remains warmer than the surrounding air, it is buoyant and tends to rise. If it becomes colder than the surrounding air, it tends to sink. This comparison is the core of stability.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Lapse Rates and Stability; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Stability

## MET-600
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** moisture, saturation, relative-humidity

What does saturation mean?

- A) Air contains as much water vapour as it can hold at that temperature
- B) Air contains no water vapour
- C) Air pressure is zero
- D) Wind speed is calm

**Answer:** A - Air contains as much water vapour as it can hold at that temperature.

**Explanation:** Saturation occurs when air is holding the maximum water vapour possible for its temperature. If saturated air cools further, water vapour may condense into cloud, fog, dew, or precipitation particles. Warmer air can hold more water vapour than colder air, so cooling is a common path to saturation.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Moisture and Saturation; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Humidity

## MET-601
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory

What does a small temperature-dew point spread indicate?

- A) Air is close to saturation, so cloud or fog may form with slight cooling
- B) Air is extremely dry
- C) Thunderstorms are impossible
- D) Wind must be calm

**Answer:** A - Air is close to saturation, so cloud or fog may form with slight cooling.

**Explanation:** The dew point is the temperature to which air must cool to become saturated, assuming pressure and moisture content stay roughly constant. If temperature and dew point are close, only a small amount of cooling is needed for condensation. This is useful for anticipating fog, low cloud, and visibility changes.

## MET-602
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** latent-heat, condensation, cloud-formation

What is latent heat?

- A) Heat absorbed or released during a phase change without a temperature change
- B) Heat from runway lights
- C) Heat measured only by altimeters
- D) Heat produced only by aircraft engines

**Answer:** A - Heat absorbed or released during a phase change without a temperature change.

**Explanation:** Water can change phase between vapour, liquid, and ice. During these changes, energy is absorbed or released even though the temperature of the substance may not change at that instant. Latent heat is important in weather because condensation releases heat into rising air, which can help clouds grow vertically.

## MET-603
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** condensation, latent-heat, instability

When water vapour condenses into liquid cloud droplets, latent heat is:

- A) Released to the surrounding air
- B) Destroyed
- C) Absorbed from the air only
- D) Converted into magnetic variation

**Answer:** A - Released to the surrounding air.

**Explanation:** Condensation releases latent heat. In a rising cloud, that released heat slows the rate at which the rising saturated air cools. This can make the parcel warmer than it otherwise would be, supporting further rising motion and cloud growth. This is one reason moist convection can become powerful.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Latent Heat and Condensation; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Stability and Clouds

## MET-604
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** evaporation, cooling, virga

When liquid water evaporates into water vapour, latent heat is:

- A) Absorbed from the surrounding air, cooling it
- B) Released and warming the air
- C) Removed from all weather processes
- D) Converted into pressure altitude

**Answer:** A - Absorbed from the surrounding air, cooling it.

**Explanation:** Evaporation requires energy. That energy is taken from the surrounding air, so evaporation cools the air. This is important in virga and precipitation shafts because evaporative cooling can make air denser and drive downdrafts, gusty outflow, turbulence, or wind shear.

## MET-605

When liquid water freezes into ice, latent heat is:

- A) Released
- B) Absorbed only
- C) Eliminated from the atmosphere
- D) Used to set cloud base

**Answer:** A - Released.

**Explanation:** Freezing releases latent heat, while melting absorbs latent heat. These energy exchanges influence cloud microphysics and storm development. In thunderstorms, repeated freezing, melting, and condensation processes help redistribute heat and support strong vertical circulations.

## MET-606
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** conditional-instability, saturated, unsaturated

What is conditional instability?

- A) Air is stable while unsaturated but unstable after it becomes saturated
- B) Air is always stable at all heights
- C) Air is always unstable regardless of moisture
- D) Air cannot form cloud

**Answer:** A - Air is stable while unsaturated but unstable after it becomes saturated.

**Explanation:** Conditional instability depends on whether rising air becomes saturated. Before saturation, the parcel cools relatively quickly. After condensation begins, latent heat release slows the cooling rate. That can make the saturated parcel warmer than its surroundings, allowing it to keep rising and build cumuliform cloud.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Conditional Instability; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Stability

- B) Air being cooled by runway paint

What is a temperature inversion?

- A) A layer where temperature increases with height
- B) A layer where pressure is zero
- C) A cloud made only of hail
- D) A wind reported as calm

**Answer:** A - A layer where temperature increases with height.

**Explanation:** Normally temperature often decreases with height in the lower atmosphere. In an inversion, warmer air sits above colder air, creating a stable layer that resists vertical mixing. Inversions can trap fog, haze, smoke, low cloud, and pollutants near the surface, creating poor visibility even when winds are light.

A cold clear night is followed by calm morning winds, smoke trapped near the ground, and poor visibility. What stability feature is likely present?

Smoke, haze, moisture, and pollutants can remain trapped near the ground. For VFR pilots, this can mean poor visibility despite otherwise quiet weather.

**Answer:** A - It adds heat to rising saturated air, helping maintain buoyancy and stronger updrafts.

## MET-618
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** phase-changes, latent-heat, learning

List the main water phase changes and whether they absorb or release latent heat.

**Answer:**
Condensation releases latent heat. Evaporation absorbs latent heat. Freezing releases latent heat. Melting absorbs latent heat. Deposition, where vapour becomes ice, releases latent heat. Sublimation, where ice becomes vapour, absorbs latent heat.

**Explanation:** These energy exchanges help drive weather. Released heat can support rising motion in clouds, while absorbed heat can cool air and contribute to downdrafts. Understanding phase changes makes icing, precipitation, fog, virga, and thunderstorm development much easier to connect.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Phase Changes of Water; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Theory

## MET-619
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** stability, cloud-types, learning

Explain why stable air tends to produce layered cloud while unstable air tends to produce cumuliform cloud.

**Answer:**
Stable air resists vertical displacement, so rising motion tends to spread out horizontally and form layers. This produces stratus-type clouds, widespread cloud decks, drizzle, or steady precipitation. Unstable air encourages rising parcels to keep rising, so cloud grows vertically into cumulus, towering cumulus, or cumulonimbus if moisture and lift are sufficient.

**Explanation:** Cloud shape is a visible clue to the atmosphere's stability. Layered cloud often points to smoother but lower-ceiling weather, while vertically developed cloud points to turbulence, showers, and possible thunderstorms. A pilot can use this clue together with reports and forecasts to anticipate flight conditions.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Stability and Cloud Types; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Clouds and Stability

## MET-620
**Topic:** Meteorology - Stability and Moisture Theory  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** integrated-theory, instability, go-no-go

The morning starts with humid air and a small temperature-dew point spread. By afternoon, strong surface heating is forecast with towering cumulus possible. What weather trend should a VFR pilot anticipate?

**Answer:** Expect increasing instability, rising cumulus development, possible showers or thunderstorms, turbulence, and changing visibility or wind near precipitation.

**Explanation:** Moist air provides fuel for cloud formation, and surface heating can make the lower atmosphere unstable. If lifting is present, rising air may reach saturation and continue growing vertically, helped by latent heat release. The day may begin quietly but become much more active by afternoon. A good VFR plan would consider earlier timing, escape routes, updated radar/satellite, and thunderstorm avoidance.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Stability, Moisture, and Thunderstorm Development; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Weather Decision-Making
