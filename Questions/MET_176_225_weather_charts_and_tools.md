## MET-178
**Topic:** Meteorology - Weather Charts and Tools  
**Type:** Image Interpretation  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** surface-analysis, pressure-systems, isobars

![Surface analysis overview](assets/MET-178_surface_analysis_overview.png)

On a surface analysis chart, what do closely spaced isobars indicate?

**Answer:** A strong pressure gradient and therefore stronger winds.

**Explanation:** Isobars connect areas of equal pressure. When they are close together, pressure changes rapidly over a short distance, producing stronger wind. For VFR pilots, tight isobars suggest crosswind, mechanical turbulence, and enroute groundspeed concerns.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 25, Surface Analysis Chart; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Weather Charts

## MET-180
**Topic:** Meteorology - Weather Charts and Tools  
**Type:** Image Interpretation  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** station-plot, pressure, decoding

![Station plot decode practice](assets/MET-180_station_plot_decode_practice.png)

On a station plot, why is the sea level pressure often coded with only the last three digits?

**Answer:** To save space on the chart; the pilot must reconstruct the full pressure value by adding either 9 or 10 in front and placing the decimal before the last digit.

**Explanation:** A plotted pressure of `132` usually means 1013.2 hPa, while `982` usually means 998.2 hPa. Choose the value that gives a realistic sea level pressure.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 25, Station Plot Models; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR Sea Level Pressure Remarks

**Topic:** Meteorology - Weather Charts and Tools  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** surface-analysis, preflight, interpretation

How can a surface analysis chart help a VFR pilot make a weather decision?

**Answer:** It helps the pilot see the broad weather pattern: highs, lows, fronts, troughs, ridges, isobars, wind patterns, and areas of significant weather. This gives context for METARs, TAFs, GFAs, radar, and satellite imagery.

What does composite reflectivity radar show?

**Answer:** The strongest radar return detected in a vertical column of the atmosphere.

**Explanation:** Composite reflectivity can reveal stronger precipitation aloft that may not appear as strongly on the lowest radar slice. It is useful for identifying convective intensity, but it can make precipitation appear more widespread or intense than what is occurring at low altitude.

![Composite versus base reflectivity](assets/MET-193_composite_vs_base_reflectivity.png)

Why should a pilot understand the difference between composite reflectivity and base reflectivity?

**Answer:** Because composite reflectivity shows the strongest return anywhere in the vertical column, while base reflectivity shows the lowest scan; each can emphasize different hazards.

**Explanation:** Composite reflectivity may highlight elevated convective cores, while base reflectivity may better represent precipitation closer to the surface. Pilots should avoid using a single radar product as the whole weather picture.

What is anomalous propagation on weather radar?

**Answer:** False or misleading radar echoes caused by unusual bending of the radar beam, often due to atmospheric refraction.

**Explanation:** Anomalous propagation can make ground targets appear as precipitation. Pilots should compare radar with satellite, METARs, PIREPs, and the larger weather situation before assuming every return is real precipitation.

Why is radar attenuation dangerous when interpreting intense precipitation?

**Answer:** Heavy precipitation can weaken or block the radar beam, hiding more severe weather behind the first strong return.

**Explanation:** An area behind a very strong cell may appear weaker or clear because the radar energy has been absorbed or scattered. This is one reason pilots should not attempt to pick through thunderstorms using radar alone.

## MET-206
**Topic:** Meteorology - Weather Charts and Tools  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** satellite, radar, comparison

Why should pilots compare satellite imagery with radar rather than using either product alone?

**Answer:** Satellite imagery shows cloud patterns and cloud-top information, while radar shows precipitation echoes. A cloud layer may not appear on radar if it has little or no precipitation, and radar may not show cloud tops.

**Explanation:** Combining products gives a more complete picture. Satellite can reveal cloud shields and convective growth, while radar helps locate precipitation intensity and movement.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 24, Radar and Satellite Observations; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Radar and Satellite Imagery
