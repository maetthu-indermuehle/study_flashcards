## Question 1 — Multiple Choice

**What are the four forces acting on an aircraft in flight?**

- A) Thrust, drag, gravity, and velocity
- B) Lift, weight, thrust, and drag
- C) Lift, momentum, thrust, and friction
- D) Bernoulli force, weight, thrust, and resistance

**Correct Answer:** B — Lift, weight, thrust, and drag

**Explanation:**
An aircraft in flight is always under the influence of four main forces. **Lift** is generated by the wings and acts upward, perpendicular to the relative airflow. **Weight** is the total gravitational force acting downward through the aircraft's Centre of Gravity. **Thrust** is the forward force produced by the engine and propeller. **Drag** is the rearward resistance of the air, acting parallel to the relative airflow and opposing forward motion. In straight-and-level unaccelerated flight, lift equals weight and thrust equals drag.

**Reference:** Transport Canada *Flight Training Manual* (TP1102E, 4th ed.), Chapter 1 — Basic Principles of Flight, p. 3–4

## Question 2 — Multiple Choice

**What happens to induced drag as airspeed increases?**

- A) Induced drag increases proportionally with airspeed
- B) Induced drag stays constant regardless of airspeed
- C) Induced drag decreases as airspeed increases
- D) Induced drag only exists during take-off and landing

**Correct Answer:** C — Induced drag decreases as airspeed increases

**Explanation:**
Induced drag is a by-product of lift. It is caused by the wing tip vortices formed when higher-pressure air beneath the wing flows around the wing tip into the lower-pressure air above. At low airspeeds, the aircraft must fly at a high angle of attack to generate sufficient lift, and this large angle of attack produces a lot of induced drag. As airspeed increases, a smaller angle of attack is needed to maintain lift, and so induced drag decreases. This is the opposite behaviour to parasite drag, which *increases* with airspeed. The speed at which the combined total drag is lowest is called the **minimum drag speed**, and it is important for gliding, range, and endurance calculations.

**Reference:** Transport Canada *Flight Training Manual* (TP1102E, 4th ed.), Chapter 1 — Basic Principles of Flight, p. 6–8

## Question 3 — Open Answer

**Explain the difference between *angle of incidence* and *angle of attack*, and why this distinction matters in flight.**

**Answer:**
The **angle of incidence** is a *fixed* structural angle — it is the angle between the wing chord line and the aircraft's longitudinal axis (the fore-aft centreline of the fuselage). It is built into the aircraft by the manufacturer and does not change in flight.

The **angle of attack (AoA)**, by contrast, is a *variable* aerodynamic angle — it is the angle between the wing chord line and the **relative airflow** (the direction the air is actually meeting the wing). Because relative airflow is always parallel and opposite to the aircraft's flight path, the angle of attack changes whenever the pilot changes pitch attitude, airspeed, or the direction of flight. It has nothing to do with the aircraft's attitude relative to the horizon.

**Why it matters:** The angle of attack directly controls the amount of lift the wing generates — up to a point. As AoA increases, lift increases. However, if AoA exceeds the **critical angle of attack**, the smooth airflow over the upper wing surface breaks down (the boundary layer separates), lift collapses, and the wing stalls. A stall can occur at *any* airspeed or attitude if the critical AoA is exceeded. This is why pilots must understand AoA and not confuse it with nose-high attitude.

**Reference:** Transport Canada *Flight Training Manual* (TP1102E, 4th ed.), Chapter 1 — Basic Principles of Flight, p. 5
