## MET-128
**Topic:** Meteorology - Canadian Weather Products  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** self-briefing, awws, cfps, fic

What is the difference between self-briefing with online Canadian aviation weather products and calling a FIC briefer?

**Answer:** Self-briefing lets a pilot review products such as METARs, TAFs, GFAs, PIREPs, radar, satellite imagery, weather charts, and NOTAMs directly. Calling a FIC briefer adds professional interpretation of the weather situation, tailored to the planned route, timing, altitude, aircraft, and pilot questions.

**Explanation:** Both methods can be useful. A good practice is to review the big weather picture first, then contact a briefer when the situation is complicated, marginal, or changing.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Aviation Weather Web Site and Weather Briefing Tips

- C) SPECIs are only used in the United States

**Topic:** Meteorology - Canadian Weather Products  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** gfa, icing, turbulence, freezing-level

What information is shown on the GFA Icing, Turbulence, and Freezing Level chart?

- A) Forecast icing and turbulence areas, their bases and tops, and freezing levels
- B) Only surface wind direction
- C) Only hourly observed weather
- D) Only airport runway condition reports

**Answer:** A - Forecast icing and turbulence areas, their bases and tops, and freezing levels

**Explanation:** This GFA chart depicts forecast moderate or severe icing and turbulence areas and expected freezing levels. It should be used with the matching Clouds and Weather chart.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Icing, Turbulence, and Freezing Level Chart

On a GFA turbulence area, what does `LLWS` identify?

## MET-172
**Topic:** Meteorology - Canadian Weather Products  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** radar, precipitation, limitations

What important limitation should a pilot remember about weather radar imagery?

- A) Radar does not show cloud cover
- B) Radar shows exact freezing levels everywhere
- C) Radar replaces all forecasts
- D) Radar shows all turbulence in clear air

**Answer:** A - Radar does not show cloud cover

**Explanation:** Weather radar is useful for identifying precipitation areas and echo tops, but it does not show all clouds. Cloud layers without precipitation may not appear on radar.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Weather Radar

## MET-173
**Topic:** Meteorology - Canadian Weather Products  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** satellite, visible, infrared

Which two common types of satellite imagery are available for Canadian aviation weather use?

- A) Visible and infrared
- B) Magnetic and pressure
- C) Rime and clear
- D) VFR and IFR

**Answer:** A - Visible and infrared

**Explanation:** Visible imagery depends on reflected sunlight and is useful during daylight. Infrared imagery senses temperature and can be useful day or night for cloud-top patterns.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Satellite Imagery

## MET-175
**Topic:** Meteorology - Canadian Weather Products  
**Type:** Scenario Based  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** preflight, interpretation, weather-products

You are planning a VFR cross-country in Canada. The departure METAR is VFR, but the GFA along the route shows `XTNSV 2-4SM -SN BKN008`, the TAF at destination has `TEMPO 1 1/2SM -SN BLSN BKN008`, and an AIRMET has been issued for widespread low ceilings and visibility. What should you conclude?

**Answer:** Although the departure airport is currently VFR, the route and destination forecast products indicate widespread marginal or IFR weather risk. For a VFR PPL flight, this should trigger a strong no-go, delay, reroute, or detailed FIC briefing decision rather than launching based only on the departure METAR.

**Explanation:** A safe weather decision uses multiple products. METARs show current point observations, while the GFA, TAF, and AIRMET show route and forecast hazards. Widespread low ceilings, reduced visibility, snow, and blowing snow are serious VFR risks.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, Weather Briefing Tips, GFA, TAF, and AIRMET; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Pilot Navigation - Weather
