## MET-016
**Original ID:** MET-016  
**Source File:** `Questions/ppl_meteorology_flashcards_batch_1_markdown.md`

**Topic:** Meteorology — Atmosphere & Pressure  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** density-altitude, performance

Explain how high temperature affects density altitude and aircraft performance.

**Answer:**  
High temperatures cause air to become less dense. As the air expands, there are fewer air molecules in a given volume, which increases density altitude. Density altitude is essentially the altitude at which the aircraft “feels” like it is operating from a performance perspective.

As density altitude increases, aircraft performance decreases significantly. The engine produces less power because less oxygen is available for combustion. The propeller becomes less efficient because it has less dense air to “bite” into. The wings also generate less lift because fewer air molecules flow over the airfoil.

Operationally, this means:
- Longer takeoff rolls
- Reduced climb rates
- Lower overall aircraft performance
- Reduced obstacle clearance capability

This effect becomes especially dangerous at high-elevation airports during hot summer days. A small aircraft operating near maximum weight may no longer be capable of climbing safely after takeoff.

Pilots must carefully calculate takeoff and climb performance whenever operating in high density altitude conditions.

**Explanation:**  
Density altitude combines the effects of pressure altitude and temperature. Even airports near sea level can experience dangerously high density altitude during very hot weather.

**Reference:** Flight Training Manual; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook Ch. 3

## MET-017
**Original ID:** MET-017  
**Source File:** `Questions/ppl_meteorology_flashcards_batch_1_markdown.md`

**Topic:** Meteorology — Temperature & Stability  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** stable-air, unstable-air

Compare stable air and unstable air.

**Answer:**  
Stable air resists vertical movement. If a parcel of air is displaced upward, it tends to sink back toward its original position. Stable air is commonly associated with:
- Smooth flying conditions
- Stratiform clouds
- Poor visibility and haze
- Continuous precipitation
- Temperature inversions

Because vertical air movement is suppressed, stable air generally produces layered clouds and calmer weather conditions.

Unstable air encourages vertical movement. If air begins to rise, it continues rising because it remains warmer than the surrounding air. Unstable air is associated with:
- Turbulence
- Strong thermals
- Good visibility
- Cumuliform clouds
- Showers and thunderstorms

Strong instability can lead to towering cumulus and cumulonimbus clouds with severe turbulence, hail, lightning, and heavy precipitation.

For pilots, atmospheric stability is important because it strongly affects ride quality, cloud development, turbulence, and thunderstorm potential.

**Explanation:**  
Atmospheric stability determines whether rising air continues to rise or sinks back down. This influences almost all weather development.

**Reference:** Flight Training Manual; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook Ch. 5

## MET-018
**Original ID:** MET-018  
**Source File:** `Questions/ppl_meteorology_flashcards_batch_1_markdown.md`

**Topic:** Meteorology — Atmosphere & Pressure  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** pressure, altimeter

Why must pilots frequently update altimeter settings during cross-country flights?

**Answer:**  
Atmospheric pressure changes continuously across geographic areas and weather systems. Since an altimeter measures pressure rather than true height directly, incorrect pressure settings cause altitude errors.

If a pilot flies into an area of lower pressure without updating the altimeter, the aircraft will actually be lower than indicated. Conversely, flying into higher pressure without updating causes the aircraft to be higher than indicated.

This is especially important:
- Near terrain
- During approaches
- In mountainous areas
- In controlled airspace where altitude separation is critical

The common memory aid is:
> “From high to low, look out below.”

Pilots update altimeter settings regularly using ATIS, AWOS, ASOS, FSS, or ATC information to maintain accurate altitude indications and ensure proper terrain and traffic separation.

**Explanation:**  
Altimeters operate by sensing atmospheric pressure. Since pressure changes naturally with weather systems, the altimeter must be recalibrated frequently during flight.

**Reference:** TC AIM MET; Flight Training Manual

## MET-019
**Original ID:** MET-019  
**Source File:** `Questions/ppl_meteorology_flashcards_batch_1_markdown.md`

**Topic:** Meteorology — Temperature & Stability  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** inversion, visibility

How can a temperature inversion affect visibility?

**Answer:**  
A temperature inversion occurs when temperature increases with altitude instead of decreasing normally. Inversions create very stable atmospheric conditions that suppress vertical air movement.

Because air cannot mix vertically, smoke, dust, haze, moisture, and pollutants become trapped near the surface. This often leads to:
- Reduced visibility
- Haze
- Smog
- Mist
- Persistent fog

Temperature inversions commonly occur:
- During clear, calm nights
- Under high-pressure systems
- In valleys
- During winter conditions

For pilots, inversions are important because visibility may be much worse near the ground than forecast conditions at higher altitudes suggest. Inversions can also hide terrain and reduce visual references during takeoff and landing.

Strong inversions may additionally produce wind shear when stronger winds exist above the inversion layer.

**Explanation:**  
Stable air inside an inversion prevents vertical mixing, trapping particles and moisture near the surface.

**Reference:** Flight Training Manual; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook Ch. 5

## MET-020

**Topic:** Meteorology — Atmosphere & Pressure  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Advanced  
**Tags:** density-altitude, mountain-flying

Why is density altitude particularly important at high-elevation airports during summer?

**Answer:**  
High-elevation airports already have lower air pressure and lower air density because atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude. During hot summer weather, temperatures further reduce air density, causing density altitude to increase dramatically.

As density altitude rises:
- Engines produce less power
- Propellers lose efficiency
- Wings generate less lift
- Climb performance decreases
- Takeoff distance increases significantly

An airport physically located at 5,000 ft elevation may behave like an 8,000 ft or higher airport on a hot day.

This creates serious operational hazards, especially:
- Short runways
- Mountainous terrain
- Heavy aircraft loading
- High outside temperatures

Pilots may discover that:
- The aircraft accelerates slowly
- Rotation occurs later
- Climb rate is poor
- Obstacle clearance becomes marginal

Many accidents occur because pilots underestimate density altitude effects or rely on sea-level performance expectations.

Proper preflight planning must include:
- Density altitude calculations
- Takeoff performance charts
- Weight reduction if necessary
- Time-of-day considerations

Flying early in the morning when temperatures are cooler often improves performance significantly.

**Explanation:**  
Density altitude is one of the most important performance considerations in mountain flying and summer operations.

**Reference:** Flight Training Manual; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook Ch. 3

**Difficulty:** Advanced

Explain the difference between geostrophic wind and surface wind.

**Answer:**
Geostrophic wind occurs high above the surface where friction is negligible.

At these altitudes:
- Pressure gradient force pushes air toward low pressure
- Coriolis force deflects moving air
- The two forces balance

As a result, geostrophic wind flows nearly parallel to isobars.

Near the Earth’s surface, friction slows the wind. This weakens the Coriolis effect and causes surface wind to cross isobars slightly toward lower pressure.

Surface winds are therefore:
- Slower
- More turbulent
- More terrain-influenced
- Angled toward low pressure

Pilots notice these differences when comparing:
- Surface winds
- Winds aloft forecasts
- Upper-level jet stream flow

**Explanation:**
Friction is the primary difference between geostrophic winds aloft and surface winds.

**Reference:** Flight Training Manual; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook Ch. 4

Explain why microbursts are extremely hazardous to aircraft.

**Answer:**
A microburst is a powerful localized downdraft produced by a thunderstorm.

As the downdraft strikes the ground, air spreads outward rapidly in all directions. Aircraft flying through a microburst may first experience an increasing headwind, followed immediately by a severe downdraft and rapidly decreasing headwind.

This sequence can cause:
- Sudden airspeed loss
- Rapid sink rates
- Loss of lift
- Inability to climb

Microbursts are especially dangerous during takeoff and landing because aircraft are already operating close to the ground with limited energy margins.

Pilots should avoid thunderstorms entirely and pay close attention to:
- LLWAS alerts
- ATC wind shear warnings
- Virga and heavy precipitation
- Rapidly changing surface winds

**Explanation:** Microbursts create extreme low-level wind shear that may exceed aircraft performance capability.
