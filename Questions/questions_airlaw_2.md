## Q101
**Topic:** Air Law — Aerodrome Operations
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What does a white dumbbell symbol on the signal square at an aerodrome indicate?

- A) The aerodrome is closed
- B) Aircraft are confined to hard surface areas for all movements
- C) Light aircraft may use the grass area
- D) The runway is wet

**Answer:** B — Aircraft are confined to hard surface areas for all movements

**Explanation:** A white dumbbell (two circles connected by a bar) means aircraft must use only paved/hard surfaces for taxiing, take-off, and landing. If the dumbbell has black bars across each end, it means the hard surface is available but the aerodrome is not fit for fixed-wing operations (e.g., due to mud or standing water on the grass).

**Reference:** CAR 602.96; TP 11919 PSTAR S.2

## Q102
**Topic:** Air Law — Aerodrome Operations
**Type:** Multiple Choice

At what wind speed does a standard dry windsock become fully extended (horizontal)?

- A) 6 knots
- B) 10 knots
- C) 15 knots or more
- D) 20 knots

**Answer:** C — 15 knots or more

**Explanation:** A standard dry windsock reacts as follows: fully horizontal = 15 kt+; 5° below horizontal = 10 kt; 30° below horizontal = 6 kt. A completely limp sock indicates very light or calm winds. The windsock also shows wind direction — aircraft should land INTO the wind (toward the open end of the sock).

**Reference:** FTM Ch.5; TP 12880

## Q103
**Topic:** Air Law — Aerodrome Operations
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What is the purpose of the circuit "crosswind" leg?

- A) To align the aircraft with the runway centerline
- B) To transition from the upwind (takeoff) direction to the downwind leg, turning 90° to the runway
- C) To check for other traffic on final approach
- D) To allow the aircraft to slow down before the base turn

**Answer:** B — To transition from the upwind (takeoff) direction to the downwind leg, turning 90° to the runway

**Explanation:** After takeoff on the upwind leg, the aircraft turns 90° onto the crosswind leg (perpendicular to the runway). Another 90° turn begins the downwind leg (parallel to the runway, opposite to the landing direction). The circuit legs in order are: upwind → crosswind → downwind → base → final.

**Reference:** FTM Ch.17

## Q111
**Topic:** Air Law — Wake Turbulence
**Type:** Multiple Choice

How long can wake turbulence vortices persist in calm (still) air conditions?

- A) Less than 30 seconds
- B) Up to 1 minute
- C) 2 minutes or more
- D) Vortices dissipate instantly once the generating aircraft departs the area

**Answer:** C — 2 minutes or more

**Explanation:** In calm or light wind conditions, vortices can persist for 2 minutes or more. The standard ATC wake turbulence separation for light aircraft following a heavy aircraft on final approach is typically 3 minutes. Never assume vortices have dissipated unless adequate time has elapsed.

## Q112
**Topic:** Air Law — Wake Turbulence
**Type:** Open Answer

A Cessna 172 pilot is instructed by ATC to land on the same runway immediately after a Boeing 737 has just touched down. What wake turbulence considerations apply and what should the pilot do?

**Answer:**
The 737 generates significant wake turbulence. As the 737 slows and touches down, its vortices sink toward the runway surface and move outward. The safest approach: request additional spacing (at least 3 minutes after a heavy jet) if possible. If cleared to follow, remain at or above the jet's approach path glidepath and touch down beyond the 737's touchdown point. Land on the upwind side of the runway centerline if a crosswind is present, to stay away from the vortex drifting downwind. If spacing cannot be ensured, execute a go-around and request extended spacing.

**Explanation:** The vortices from the heavier aircraft will roll along the runway surface and can cause the following light aircraft to roll violently. Landing beyond the heavy aircraft's touchdown point ensures the Cessna lands past the area where the vortices have settled. A go-around is always the safest option if in doubt.

**Reference:** TP 11919 PSTAR S.7

## Q122
**Topic:** Air Law — Regulations
**Type:** Open Answer

Explain the difference between "flight time" and "air time" as used in the Canadian aviation system.

**Answer:**
Flight time is the total period of time from the moment the aircraft first moves under its own power for the purpose of flight until it comes to rest at the end of the flight. This includes taxiing before and after the flight. Air time is the time elapsed between the aircraft leaving the surface on take-off until it touches the surface again on landing — i.e., actual time airborne. Journey log entries typically record flight time. Air time is shorter than flight time (no taxi included). Flight time is generally what counts toward licence requirements.

**Explanation:** This distinction matters for log entries and licence calculations. A pilot who does a 30-minute taxi before a 1-hour flight has more flight time than air time. Transport Canada licence requirements specify flight time, not air time.

**Reference:** FTM Ch.4; CAR 101.01

## Q130
**Topic:** Air Law — Regulations
**Type:** Open Answer

What are Airworthiness Directives (ADs) and what must a pilot know about them?

**Answer:**
Airworthiness Directives are mandatory safety notices issued by Transport Canada (or a foreign authority for an imported aircraft type) that address known or suspected safety defects in aircraft, engines, propellers, or components. ADs specify: what must be done (inspection, modification, replacement), when it must be done (within a specific flight hours limit or calendar time), and who may perform the work (usually a licensed AME). Compliance is not optional — failure to comply with an applicable AD makes the aircraft unairworthy and illegal to fly. Pilots should check with the AME or aircraft owner that all ADs have been complied with. The status of AD compliance is recorded in the aircraft journey log and technical records.

**Explanation:** As PIC, the pilot bears ultimate responsibility for the aircraft's airworthiness before every flight. While AMEs perform the actual AD work, the pilot should understand that ADs exist and know how to verify compliance with the aircraft owner or operator.

**Reference:** CAR 605.84; FTM Ch.4

## Q138
**Topic:** Air Law — VFR Minimums
**Type:** Multiple Choice

A VFR pilot is flying at 800 ft AGL in Class G uncontrolled airspace. A cloud layer is 400 ft above the aircraft. Is the pilot in compliance?

- A) Yes — at 800 ft AGL the pilot only needs to be clear of cloud
- B) No — the pilot must remain 1,000 ft above any cloud layer
- C) Yes — the 1,000 ft above cloud rule only applies in controlled airspace
- D) No — at 800 ft AGL the pilot must be at least 500 ft below cloud

**Answer:** A — Yes — at 800 ft AGL the pilot only needs to be clear of cloud

**Explanation:** At or below 700 ft AGL in Class G airspace, the requirement is just 1 SM visibility and clear of cloud. At 800 ft AGL (above 700 ft AGL but below 1,000 ft AGL, which is in the "above 700 ft" category), the requirements are: 1 SM visibility, 500 ft below cloud, 1,000 ft above cloud, 2,000 ft horizontal. ## Q190
**Topic:** Air Law — Regulations
**Type:** Open Answer

What is a VFR pilot's correct course of action if they encounter deteriorating weather en route and are unable to maintain VFR?

**Answer:**
(1) Immediately execute a 180° turn back to VFR conditions — this is the first and best option. Do not continue into IMC hoping to break out. (2) If terrain or other hazards prevent a 180° turn, look for a suitable landing area and land as soon as practicable. (3) Contact ATC (on the assigned frequency or 121.5 MHz) and report the situation — request assistance such as radar vectors to VFR conditions, weather information for alternate routes, or guidance to the nearest suitable aerodrome. **Explanation:** Statistics consistently show that VFR flight into IMC is one of the most common and fatal accident scenarios. A non-instrument-rated pilot who enters IMC has an average of about 178 seconds before losing control of the aircraft. Early action — turning back at the first sign of deteriorating weather — is the critical safety behavior.

**Reference:** FTM Ch.6; TC Safety publications
