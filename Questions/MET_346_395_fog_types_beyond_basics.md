## MET-346
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** fog, definition, visibility

What is fog?

- A) A cloud based at the Earth's surface that reduces horizontal visibility
- B) Ice crystals only above 20,000 ft
- C) Any precipitation falling from cloud
- D) Smoke produced by forest fires

**Answer:** A - A cloud based at the Earth's surface that reduces horizontal visibility.

**Explanation:** Fog is essentially cloud at ground level. It consists of tiny water droplets, ice crystals, or both suspended near the surface. For pilots, the key issue is not only the textbook definition but the operational effect: fog reduces horizontal visibility, may obscure the horizon, may hide terrain and obstacles, and can create very low ceilings or vertical visibility. Fog differs from a normal cloud mainly because its base is at the surface.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Fog; NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Present Weather

## MET-347
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** fog, formation, dewpoint

Fog usually forms when:

- A) Air temperature and dew point become equal or nearly equal
- B) Pressure altitude equals density altitude
- C) Wind speed exceeds 50 kt
- D) Air becomes completely dry

**Answer:** A - Air temperature and dew point become equal or nearly equal.

**Explanation:** Fog forms when air near the surface becomes saturated. This can happen by cooling the air to its dew point, as in radiation, advection, or upslope fog, or by adding moisture to the air, as in precipitation-induced fog or steam fog. A small temperature-dew point spread is therefore an important warning sign, but it does not tell the whole story. Wind, terrain, cloud cover, surface moisture, and air mass movement all matter.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Fog; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 6, Water Vapor

## MET-348
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** fog, mist, br, fg

In aviation weather reports, what is the practical difference between `FG` and `BR`?

- A) `FG` is fog and `BR` is mist
- B) `FG` is freezing rain and `BR` is blowing rain
- C) `FG` is forecast gusts and `BR` is broken runway
- D) There is no difference

**Answer:** A - `FG` is fog and `BR` is mist.

**Explanation:** Fog and mist are both visibility restrictions caused by suspended water droplets, but fog is the denser restriction. In METAR/TAF coding, `FG` is used for fog when visibility is reduced below the fog threshold, while `BR` is mist and represents less severe water-droplet visibility restriction. For a pilot, this distinction matters because `BR` may still be legal VFR in some situations, while `FG` often signals very poor visibility and possible low or obscured ceilings.

**Reference:** NAV CANADA Aviation Weather Services Guide, METAR/SPECI Present Weather; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Fog and Mist

## MET-349
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** fog, radiation-fog, advection-fog, upslope-fog

Which fog types form primarily by cooling air to its dew point?

- A) Radiation fog, advection fog, and upslope fog
- B) Steam fog and frontal fog only
- C) Ice fog only
- D) Smoke and haze

**Answer:** A - Radiation fog, advection fog, and upslope fog.

**Explanation:** These fog types are grouped by their formation mechanism. Radiation fog forms when the ground cools at night and chills the air near the surface. Advection fog forms when moist air moves over a colder surface and is cooled from below. Upslope fog forms when moist stable air is forced uphill and cools adiabatically. In all three cases, cooling brings the air to saturation.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Fog Types

## MET-350
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Which fog types form primarily by adding moisture to the air?

- A) Steam fog and precipitation-induced fog
- B) Radiation fog only
- C) Advection fog only
- D) Upslope fog only

**Answer:** A - Steam fog and precipitation-induced fog.

**Explanation:** Steam fog forms when cold air moves over warmer water and moisture evaporates into the cold air, quickly saturating it. Precipitation-induced fog, also called frontal fog, forms when precipitation falls into cooler air below and evaporates or sublimates, adding moisture until the air becomes saturated. These are different from fog types caused mainly by cooling the air mass.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Steam Fog and Frontal Fog

## MET-351
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** upslope-fog, stable-air, terrain

Upslope fog forms when:

- A) Moist stable air is forced uphill and cools to its dew point
- B) Dry air descends a mountain and warms
- C) Cold air passes over warm water
- D) Fog burns off after sunrise

**Answer:** A - Moist stable air is forced uphill and cools to its dew point.

**Explanation:** As air is forced to rise along sloping terrain, pressure decreases and the air expands and cools. If the air is moist and stable, this cooling can bring it to saturation and produce fog or low stratus along the slope. Upslope fog can occur even with cloud cover, so it is not just a clear-night phenomenon like radiation fog. It can be dense, extensive, and persistent because the terrain and wind continue feeding the lifting process.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Upslope Fog; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 16, Mountain Weather

Why is upslope fog particularly important for pilots operating near rising terrain?

- A) It can obscure slopes, ridges, passes, and destination airports while nearby lower terrain remains clearer
- B) It only forms over oceans
- C) It improves mountain visibility
- D) It always indicates calm air

**Answer:** A - It can obscure slopes, ridges, passes, and destination airports while nearby lower terrain remains clearer.

## MET-356
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice

**Tags:** steam-fog, evaporation-fog, cold-air

Steam fog forms when:

- A) Cold air moves over warmer water and moisture evaporates into the air
- B) Warm dry air moves over hot desert terrain
- C) Air descends a mountain slope and warms
- D) Cloud tops reach the tropopause

**Answer:** A - Cold air moves over warmer water and moisture evaporates into the air.

**Explanation:** Steam fog is sometimes called evaporation fog because moisture is added from the warm water surface into colder air above it. The cold air quickly becomes saturated, and wisps or streamers of condensed moisture rise from the surface, looking like steam. It is common over lakes, rivers, and coastal water when cold air moves across relatively warmer water.

**Answer:** A - The air is heated and moistened from below, creating a shallow unstable layer.

## MET-361
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** ice-fog, arctic-weather, cold-temperature

Ice fog is composed primarily of:

- A) Suspended ice crystals
- B) Blowing sand
- C) Smoke particles only
- D) Liquid rain drops falling from cumulonimbus

**Answer:** A - Suspended ice crystals.

**Explanation:** Ice fog is fog made of tiny ice crystals suspended in the air. It is most associated with very cold temperatures, especially in arctic or subarctic regions. Unlike freezing fog, which is made of supercooled liquid droplets that freeze on contact with surfaces, ice fog is already in the ice-crystal phase. Both can restrict visibility severely, but their physical makeup and surface effects differ.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Fog; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 21, Arctic Weather

What is the key difference between ice fog and freezing fog?

- A) Ice fog is made of ice crystals; freezing fog is made of supercooled liquid droplets
- B) Freezing fog only occurs in summer
- C) Ice fog never reduces visibility
- D) There is no physical difference

**Answer:** A - Ice fog is made of ice crystals; freezing fog is made of supercooled liquid droplets.

**Explanation:** This distinction matters operationally. Freezing fog can deposit rime ice on aircraft, runways, windscreens, antennas, and other exposed surfaces because supercooled droplets freeze on contact. How is steam fog different from advection fog?

- B) Steam fog forms only over deserts
- C) Advection fog is made of smoke
- D) Steam fog never affects pilots

**Answer:** A - Steam fog forms when cold air moves over warmer water; advection fog forms when moist air moves over a colder surface.

## MET-386

Why does radiation fog often dissipate after sunrise?

- D) Fog changes into volcanic ash

**Answer:** A - Solar heating warms the surface and lower air, increasing the temperature-dew point spread.

## MET-394
**Topic:** Meteorology - Fog Types Beyond Basics  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** fog, comparison, learning

Compare upslope fog, steam fog, ice fog, and precipitation-induced fog in one or two sentences each.

**Answer:** Upslope fog forms when moist stable air is forced uphill and cools to saturation. Steam fog forms when cold air moves over warmer water and gains moisture. Ice fog consists of tiny ice crystals and is most common in very cold arctic or subarctic conditions. Precipitation-induced fog forms when rain or snow evaporates or sublimates into cooler air below, adding moisture until it saturates.

**Explanation:** These fog types are best learned by their formation mechanism. Upslope fog is a terrain-lift problem. Steam fog is a cold-air-over-warm-water problem. Ice fog is a very-cold-air ice-crystal problem. Precipitation fog is a falling-precipitation-moistening-cool-air problem. Knowing the mechanism helps a pilot predict where the fog may form, how long it may last, and what other hazards may accompany it.

**Reference:** FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 18, Fog Types; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 21, Arctic Weather
