## Q201
**Topic:** Air Law — Night VFR
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Under Canadian Aviation Regulations, what is the definition of "night" for the purposes of flight rules?

- A) The period from sunset to sunrise
- B) The period from the end of evening civil twilight to the beginning of morning civil twilight
- C) The period from 30 minutes after sunset to 30 minutes before sunrise
- D) The period when the sun is more than 6° below the horizon

**Answer:** B — The period from the end of evening civil twilight to the beginning of morning civil twilight

**Explanation:** Under CAR 101.01, "night" is defined as the period between the end of evening civil twilight and the beginning of morning civil twilight. Civil twilight ends/begins when the centre of the sun is 6° below the horizon. This is an important distinction — it is not simply sunset to sunrise, and it is different from the aeronautical definition used in some other countries. Pilots must know this definition because it affects lighting requirements, recency requirements, and the applicability of night-specific regulations.

**Reference:** CAR 101.01; Transport Canada AIM

## Q202
**Topic:** Air Law — Night VFR
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What aircraft lighting is required for flight at night under VFR in Canada?

- A) Navigation lights and a landing light
- B) Navigation lights, an anti-collision light, and a landing light
- C) Navigation lights and an anti-collision light
- D) Anti-collision light only

**Answer:** C — Navigation lights and an anti-collision light

**Explanation:** Under CAR 605.16 and 605.17, an aircraft operated at night must display navigation lights (red on left, green on right, white on tail) and an anti-collision light (strobe or rotating beacon). A landing light is not mandated by the CARs for night VFR flight generally, though it is strongly recommended and may be required by specific operators. Floatplanes operating on water at night have additional requirements.

**Reference:** CAR 605.16; CAR 605.17

## Q203
**Topic:** Air Law — Night VFR
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What recency requirement must a pilot meet to carry passengers at night?

- A) 5 night circuits in the preceding 6 months
- B) 5 night take-offs and 5 night landings in the preceding 6 months
- C) 5 take-offs and 5 landings at night in the preceding 12 months
- D) No specific night recency requirement beyond the general 5 take-off and landing requirement

**Answer:** B — 5 night take-offs and 5 night landings in the preceding 6 months

**Explanation:** Under CAR 401.05(2), to act as PIC of an aircraft carrying passengers at night, a pilot must have carried out at least 5 take-offs and 5 landings as the sole manipulator of the controls at night during the 6 months preceding the flight. This is in addition to the general recency requirement of 5 take-offs and 5 landings in the preceding 6 months (which may be done day or night). Night recency must be done specifically during night hours as defined in CAR 101.01.

**Reference:** CAR 401.05(2)

## Q204
**Topic:** Air Law — Night VFR
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What are the minimum VFR weather conditions required for night flight in Class G uncontrolled airspace at or below 1,000 ft AGL?

- A) 1 mile visibility, clear of cloud
- B) 2 miles visibility, 500 ft below, 1,000 ft above, and 2,000 ft horizontal from cloud
- C) 3 miles visibility, clear of cloud
- D) 1 mile visibility, 500 ft below and 1,000 ft above cloud

**Answer:** C — 3 miles visibility, clear of cloud

**Explanation:** For night flight in Class G airspace at or below 1,000 ft AGL, the VFR weather minima are 3 miles visibility and clear of cloud. This is more restrictive than the daytime minimum of 1 mile visibility and clear of cloud at the same altitude, reflecting the reduced ability to see and avoid terrain and other aircraft at night. Pilots must be aware of these night-specific weather minimums when planning night VFR flights.

**Reference:** CAR 602.115; TP 12880 PALAW

## Q205
**Topic:** Air Law — Night VFR
**Type:** Open Answer

What special equipment is required for night VFR flight in Canada, and why is each item important?

**Answer:**
For night VFR flight in Canada, CAR 605.14–605.17 require the following in addition to the standard day VFR equipment:

1. **Navigation lights** (CAR 605.16) — red port (left) light, green starboard (right) light, and white tail light. Allow other aircraft and ground observers to determine the aircraft's position and direction of travel.

2. **Anti-collision light** (CAR 605.17) — a rotating beacon or strobe system visible from all angles. Makes the aircraft conspicuous to other airspace users at night.

3. **Landing light** — required for commercial operations; strongly recommended for all night operations to illuminate the runway threshold during landing.

4. **Spare fuses or circuit breakers** — CAR 605.14 requires a set of spare fuses of each type accessible to the pilot in flight, to allow in-flight rectification of blown fuses affecting essential equipment.

5. **Flashlight (torch)** — required to enable the pilot to read instruments and charts if cockpit lighting fails.

In addition, the aircraft must have an electrical system capable of operating the required lights, and the pilot must hold an appropriate night-rating endorsement (or have met night-flying experience requirements under a PPL/CPL).

**Reference:** CAR 605.14–605.17; CAR 421.26 (Night Rating)

## Q206
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace — Class B
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Which of the following correctly describes Canadian Class B airspace?

- A) Uncontrolled airspace where IFR and VFR flight are permitted without a clearance
- B) Controlled airspace from the surface to 12,500 ft ASL surrounding Class A airspace, open to IFR and VFR without clearance
- C) High-level controlled airspace above FL 230 open to IFR with ATC clearance and VFR with ATC clearance
- D) Controlled airspace from FL 180 to FL 600 where only IFR is permitted

**Answer:** C — High-level controlled airspace above FL 230 open to IFR with ATC clearance and VFR with ATC clearance

**Explanation:** Class B airspace in Canada exists from FL 230 upward (below Class A which starts at FL 600). Both IFR and VFR flights are permitted in Class B, but both require an ATC clearance and must maintain two-way radio communication. VFR cruising altitudes and VFR weather minima applicable to Class B differ from lower-level rules. This structure is different from FAA Class B (which is the busy terminal airspace around major airports in the USA).

**Reference:** CAR 601.02; TP 12880 PALAW; Transport Canada AIM RAC 2.0

## Q207
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace — Class C
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What are the VFR requirements to enter Class C airspace?

- A) No requirement — Class C is uncontrolled and open to all
- B) Two-way radio communication must be established with ATC before entry; ATC clearance is not required
- C) ATC clearance required; two-way radio communication required; VFR weather minima of 3 miles and 500-1000-2000 cloud clearances apply
- D) ATC clearance required; transponder with Mode C required; VFR weather minima of 5 miles apply

**Answer:** C — ATC clearance required; two-way radio communication required; VFR weather minima of 3 miles and 500-1000-2000 cloud clearances apply

**Explanation:** Class C airspace (typically the terminal control area around major airports like Toronto Pearson, Vancouver, and Montreal) requires that a VFR pilot obtain an ATC clearance and establish two-way radio communication before entering. A transponder with Mode C altitude reporting is also required. The standard VFR weather minima for Class C airspace are 3 miles visibility with cloud clearances of 500 ft below, 1,000 ft above, and 2,000 ft horizontally. Pilots should contact the appropriate terminal control unit well before reaching the boundary.

**Reference:** CAR 601.04; CAR 602.114; Transport Canada AIM RAC 2.6

## Q208
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace — Class D
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What is required for a VFR aircraft to enter Class D airspace?

- A) ATC clearance and transponder with Mode C
- B) Only two-way radio communication; a clearance is not required but an acknowledgement from ATC is needed
- C) No requirements — Class D is uncontrolled
- D) A Special VFR clearance in all conditions

**Answer:** B — Only two-way radio communication; a clearance is not required but an acknowledgement from ATC is needed

**Explanation:** For VFR flight in Class D airspace (typically the control zone around smaller controlled aerodromes), the pilot must establish two-way radio communication with ATC, but does not require a formal clearance to enter. The pilot must make initial contact and receive an acknowledgement (which may be "Stand by" — meaning the aircraft cannot enter until further clearance) before entering. If ATC does not respond, the pilot must not enter Class D. A transponder with Mode C is required unless an exemption applies.

**Reference:** CAR 601.05; Transport Canada AIM RAC 2.7

## Q209
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace — Class E and G
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Which statement correctly describes the difference between Class E and Class G airspace in Canada?

- A) Class E is controlled; Class G is uncontrolled. IFR flights in Class E are under ATC control; VFR flights require no clearance in either.
- B) Class E and Class G are both uncontrolled; the only difference is the altitude.
- C) Class G is controlled airspace surrounding airports; Class E is uncontrolled en-route airspace.
- D) Both Class E and Class G require ATC clearance for IFR but not for VFR.

**Answer:** A — Class E is controlled; Class G is uncontrolled. IFR flights in Class E are under ATC control; VFR flights require no clearance in either.

**Explanation:** Class E airspace is controlled airspace, meaning IFR flights operating within it are under ATC control and separation. However, VFR pilots do not require a clearance to fly in Class E — they simply comply with the applicable VFR weather minima (3 miles visibility, 500 ft below / 1,000 ft above / 2,000 ft horizontal from cloud above 1,000 ft AGL). Class G is uncontrolled airspace where neither IFR nor VFR flights are subject to ATC control, though IFR pilots still file flight plans and operate to instrument rules. The vast majority of low-level Canadian rural airspace is Class G.

**Reference:** CAR 601.07; Transport Canada AIM RAC 2.9

## Q212
**Topic:** Air Law — Airspace — Special Use
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What is a Mandatory Frequency (MF) area and what are a pilot's communication obligations when operating within one?

- A) An area where pilots must use a specific frequency assigned to a terminal control unit
- B) A designated area around certain aerodromes where pilots must transmit position reports and intentions on a specified frequency
- C) A frequency that all aircraft must monitor at all times below 10,000 ft ASL
- D) A requirement to contact Class D control before entering the zone

**Answer:** B — A designated area around certain aerodromes where pilots must transmit position reports and intentions on a specified frequency

**Explanation:** A Mandatory Frequency (MF) is established at certain aerodromes that have aviation weather observations but no control tower. Pilots operating within the MF area must broadcast their position and intentions on the designated frequency when: taking off, joining the circuit, on each leg of the circuit, on final approach, when clear of the active runway, and when backtracking. Position reports are mandatory, not advisory.

## Q214
**Topic:** Air Law — Altimeter Setting
**Type:** Multiple Choice

When should a pilot use QNH as the altimeter setting?

- A) When flying above the transition altitude (18,000 ft in Canada)
- B) When flying at or below the transition altitude (18,000 ft in Canada)
- C) When flying in instrument meteorological conditions only
- D) QNH is not used in Canada — only QNE (standard pressure 29.92 in. Hg) is used

**Answer:** B — When flying at or below the transition altitude (18,000 ft in Canada)

**Explanation:** In Canada, QNH (the altimeter setting adjusted to show altitude above sea level at the aerodrome) is used for all flight at or below 17,999 ft ASL. Above the transition altitude (18,000 ft), pilots set standard pressure (29.92 in. Hg or 1013.25 hPa), and altitude is then expressed as a Flight Level (FL). The transition altitude in Canada is 18,000 ft ASL, which is also the base of Class A airspace. The current altimeter setting (QNH) is obtained from ATIS, ATC, or AWOS.

**Reference:** CAR 602.35; Transport Canada AIM AIR 2.8

## Q215
**Topic:** Air Law — Altimeter Setting
**Type:** Multiple Choice

A pilot is flying cross-country and passes out of the coverage area for the local altimeter setting. What altimeter setting should be used?

- A) The standard pressure setting of 29.92 in. Hg
- B) The last received altimeter setting for the area
- C) The destination airport's current altimeter setting obtained by radio
- D) The setting from the nearest FSS or weather source available

**Answer:** D — The setting from the nearest FSS or weather source available

**Explanation:** When flying cross-country, the pilot should update the altimeter setting by obtaining the current setting from the nearest available source — typically a Flight Service Station (FSS), ATIS, AWOS, or ATC facility. An out-of-date altimeter setting can lead to significant altitude error, especially if the surface pressure is changing rapidly. Using the last received setting is acceptable as a bridge but should be updated as soon as a new source is within range. Standard pressure (29.92) should not be used below 18,000 ft ASL in Canada.

**Reference:** CAR 602.35; Transport Canada AIM AIR 2.8

## Q216
**Topic:** Air Law — Cruising Altitudes
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Under VFR cruising altitude rules, a pilot flying a magnetic track of 270° at 5,500 ft ASL in uncontrolled airspace — is this correct?

- A) Yes — westbound VFR is flown at odd thousands plus 500 ft
- B) No — westbound VFR is flown at even thousands plus 500 ft, so 4,500 ft or 6,500 ft should be used
- C) Yes — 5,500 ft is valid for any magnetic track
- D) No — VFR aircraft must fly at odd thousands only (without the 500 ft addition)

**Answer:** B — No — westbound VFR is flown at even thousands plus 500 ft, so 4,500 ft or 6,500 ft should be used

**Explanation:** Under CAR 602.34, VFR cruising altitudes in uncontrolled airspace above 3,000 ft AGL are:
- Magnetic track 000°–179° (northeasterly): odd thousands plus 500 ft (3,500 / 5,500 / 7,500 ft, etc.)
- Magnetic track 180°–359° (southwesterly): even thousands plus 500 ft (4,500 / 6,500 / 8,500 ft, etc.)

A magnetic track of 270° is westbound (southwesterly sector), so the correct VFR cruising altitudes are even thousands plus 500 ft — i.e., 4,500 or 6,500 ft, not 5,500 ft. Flying 5,500 ft westbound would violate CAR 602.34.

**Reference:** CAR 602.34; TP 12880 PALAW

## Q217
**Topic:** Air Law — Cruising Altitudes
**Type:** Multiple Choice

The VFR cruising altitude rules in CAR 602.34 apply above which altitude?

- A) 1,000 ft AGL
- B) 3,000 ft AGL
- C) 3,000 ft ASL
- D) Above the transition altitude of 18,000 ft ASL

**Answer:** B — 3,000 ft AGL

**Explanation:** The VFR cruising altitude rules (odd/even thousands plus 500 ft) apply when flying more than 3,000 ft above ground level (AGL). Below 3,000 ft AGL, there is no mandated cruising altitude under CAR 602.34, though pilots should still use right-of-way rules and apply safe practices. Note the reference is AGL (above ground level), not ASL (above sea level), which makes the rule terrain-following — if terrain rises, the altitude at which the rule kicks in also rises.

**Reference:** CAR 602.34

## Q218
**Topic:** Air Law — Right of Way — Specific Rules
**Type:** Multiple Choice

An aircraft towing a glider is converging with a powered aircraft at the same altitude. Which has right of way?

- A) The powered aircraft, because it is more manoeuvrable
- B) The towing aircraft, because it has restricted manoeuvrability
- C) Neither — both must manoeuvre to avoid collision
- D) The one on the right

**Answer:** B — The towing aircraft, because it has restricted manoeuvrability

**Explanation:** Under CAR 602.19, the right-of-way rules specify that aircraft with greater manoeuvrability must give way to aircraft with lesser manoeuvrability. An aircraft towing another aircraft (such as a glider tow) has significantly restricted manoeuvrability — it cannot easily turn or climb/descend without endangering the glider on the tow rope. Therefore, the powered aircraft overtaking or converging with a towing aircraft must give way. This general principle also explains why powered aircraft give way to gliders, which give way only to balloons and airships.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19

## Q219
**Topic:** Air Law — Right of Way — Specific Rules
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Two aircraft are approaching the same aerodrome for landing. Aircraft A is higher. What is the right-of-way rule?

- A) Aircraft A has right of way because it is higher
- B) Aircraft A must give way to Aircraft B because Aircraft B is lower and closer to landing
- C) The aircraft that made the earlier radio call has right of way
- D) The aircraft with more passengers has right of way

**Answer:** B — Aircraft A must give way to Aircraft B because Aircraft B is lower and closer to landing

**Explanation:** Under CAR 602.19(5), when two aircraft are approaching an aerodrome to land, the higher aircraft must give way to the lower aircraft. This rule ensures that the aircraft already established lower in the circuit or on approach is not disrupted. However, this rule does not allow the lower aircraft to cut in front of an aircraft that is already on final approach — the intent is that aircraft higher in the circuit yield to those already established lower. Pilots must use common sense as well as the rules.

**Reference:** CAR 602.19(5)

## Q222
**Topic:** Air Law — Low Flying
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What is the minimum altitude for flight over a congested area of a city or town, or over an open-air assembly of persons?

- A) 300 ft AGL
- B) 500 ft AGL
- C) 1,000 ft AGL
- D) 1,500 ft AGL

**Answer:** C — 1,000 ft AGL

**Explanation:** Under CAR 602.14, no person shall operate an aircraft over a congested area of a city, town, or settlement, or over an open-air assembly of persons, at an altitude of less than 1,000 ft above the highest obstacle within a horizontal distance of 2,000 ft from the aircraft. This rule ensures a minimum safety margin for persons and property on the ground in densely populated areas, and provides enough altitude for an emergency landing attempt if the engine fails. For non-congested areas, the minimum is 500 ft from any person, vessel, vehicle, or structure.

**Reference:** CAR 602.14(2)

## Q224
**Topic:** Air Law — Emergency Transponder Codes
**Type:** Multiple Choice

A pilot has experienced a complete radio failure. Which transponder code should be selected?

- A) 7500
- B) 7600
- C) 7700
- D) 7000

**Answer:** B — 7600

**Explanation:** The emergency transponder codes are standardized:
- **7500** — Unlawful interference (hijacking)
- **7600** — Radio communications failure (NORDO)
- **7700** — General emergency

If a pilot experiences radio failure, squawking 7600 alerts ATC to the NORDO condition so they can provide appropriate assistance — typically by establishing a light-gun signal communication with the aircraft if it intends to land at a controlled aerodrome. The pilot should also attempt to re-establish communication by all available means and should not squawk 7700 unless there is also a genuine emergency threatening the safety of the aircraft.

**Reference:** CAR 605.38; Transport Canada AIM COM 5.14; TP 11919 PSTAR S.3

## Q225

**Type:** Multiple Choice

If an aircraft is being hijacked, what transponder code should be selected?

- A) 7500
- B) 7600
- C) 7700

**Answer:** A — 7500

**Explanation:** Transponder code **7500** is the international code for unlawful interference — i.e., hijacking. Pilots are trained never to inadvertently select this code, as it will trigger immediate security responses. The three emergency codes — 7500, 7600, 7700 — should be memorized by all pilots as part of emergency procedures training.

## Q227
**Topic:** Air Law — Aviation Occurrences
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Under the Aeronautics Act and the Canadian Aviation Regulations, who is responsible for investigating aviation accidents in Canada?

- A) Transport Canada's Civil Aviation Division
- B) The Transportation Safety Board of Canada (TSB)
- C) The RCMP
- D) The Insurance Bureau of Canada

**Answer:** B — The Transportation Safety Board of Canada (TSB)

**Explanation:** The Transportation Safety Board of Canada (TSB) is the independent federal agency responsible for investigating aviation accidents and serious incidents in Canada. The TSB's mandate is to advance transportation safety — it does not assign blame or liability, and its investigation reports cannot be used in legal proceedings (with limited exceptions). Transport Canada may conduct a separate regulatory investigation. The TSB must be notified of aviation occurrences as prescribed by the TSB Act.

**Reference:** Transportation Safety Board Act; CAR 303.12; TP 11919 PSTAR S.14

## Q228
**Topic:** Air Law — Aviation Occurrences
**Type:** Multiple Choice

What is the definition of an "aviation accident" for TSB reporting purposes?

- A) Any unplanned landing away from a designated aerodrome
- B) An occurrence associated with the operation of an aircraft that results in a fatality or serious injury, or significant aircraft or property damage
- C) Any incident that results in an insurance claim
- D) An unscheduled maintenance event caused by in-flight damage

**Answer:** B — An occurrence associated with the operation of an aircraft that results in a fatality or serious injury, or significant aircraft or property damage

**Explanation:** Under the Transportation Safety Board Regulations, an aviation accident is an occurrence associated with the operation of an aircraft between the time any person boards the aircraft with the intention of flight and the time all such persons have disembarked, in which: (a) a person is fatally or seriously injured; (b) the aircraft sustains damage or structural failure that adversely affects structural strength, performance, or flight characteristics; or (c) the aircraft is missing or inaccessible. Accidents must be reported to the TSB immediately (by the quickest means). Incidents (less severe occurrences with safety significance) must also be reported.

**Reference:** Transportation Safety Board Regulations, SOR/92-446; CAR 303.12

Within what time frame must an aviation occurrence be reported to the TSB?

- B) Within 7 days of the occurrence
- C) Immediately (as soon as possible after the occurrence)

## Q232
**Topic:** Air Law — Alcohol and Drugs
**Type:** Open Answer

Explain the "8 hours bottle to throttle" rule and its limitations as a safety measure for pilot alcohol use.

**Answer:**
CAR 602.03 prohibits a pilot from acting as a flight crew member within **8 hours of consuming an alcoholic beverage**, or if their blood alcohol concentration (BAC) is **0.04% or more**, or while under the influence of alcohol. This is commonly summarized as "8 hours bottle to throttle."

**Limitations of the 8-hour rule:**
The 8-hour rule is a legal minimum, not a safety guarantee. Alcohol can impair cognitive function, judgment, reaction time, and spatial orientation well beyond 8 hours depending on the quantity consumed. Research shows that:
- A BAC of 0.04% — the legal limit — can still cause measurable impairment
- Larger quantities of alcohol require much longer than 8 hours to metabolize to below 0.04% BAC
- Hangovers caused by dehydration and metabolic by-products can themselves impair performance even after BAC returns to zero

**Best practice:** Transport Canada recommends 24 hours from the last drink before flying, especially after significant alcohol consumption. Many operators require 12 or 24 hours under their Operations Manuals. Pilots should also consider the self-assessment rule under CAR 602.02 — if there is any doubt about fitness, the pilot must not fly.

**Reference:** CAR 602.02; CAR 602.03; Transport Canada Flight Training Manual

## Q248
**Topic:** Air Law — Wake Turbulence
**Type:** Multiple Choice

Where are the wingtip vortices from a large aircraft most dangerous for a following aircraft on approach?

- A) At high altitude, directly behind the generating aircraft
- B) Below and behind the generating aircraft, particularly near the threshold when following on the same approach path
- C) Above and ahead of the generating aircraft
- D) The vortices are only dangerous during take-off, not during approach

**Answer:** B — Below and behind the generating aircraft, particularly near the threshold when following on the same approach path

**Explanation:** Wingtip vortices are generated from the moment lift is produced and continue until the aircraft touches down (specifically, from rotation to touchdown). This allows the fovea to register each sector and improves detection of aircraft on collision courses, which appear as a point that does not move relative to the background.

**Explanation:** The human retina contains two types of photoreceptors: **cones** (concentrated in the fovea, responsible for colour and fine detail, require relatively bright light) and **rods** (concentrated in the periphery, sensitive to low light levels but not colour). How long should a pilot wait after scuba diving before flying, and why?

- A) 1 hour — to allow the pressure change to equalize in the ears
- B) 12 hours after dives not requiring a decompression stop; 24 hours after dives requiring decompression stops or after multiple dives

**Explanation:** Scuba diving increases the amount of dissolved nitrogen in the body's tissues. The pressure reduction in an aircraft cabin is equivalent to a rapid altitude change. Aviation medicine guidelines recommend: **12 hours** after a non-decompression-stop dive or single dive; **24 hours** after decompression-stop dives, multiple dives over consecutive days, or any dive that caused DCS symptoms.

A pilot has a head cold with nasal congestion before a planned flight. What are the risks and the appropriate course of action?
