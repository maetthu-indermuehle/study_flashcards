## MET-446
**Topic:** Meteorology - Icing Gaps  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** carburettor-icing, engine, recognition

What is carburettor icing?

- A) Ice forming inside the carburettor because fuel vaporization and pressure drop cool the air
- B) Ice forming only on the outside of the windshield
- C) Ice forming only when outside air temperature is below -20 C
- D) Ice forming only in fuel tanks after refuelling

**Answer:** A - Ice forming inside the carburettor because fuel vaporization and pressure drop cool the air.

**Explanation:** In a carburettor, air speeds up through the venturi and fuel vaporizes into that airflow. Both processes can lower the temperature in the carburettor throat enough for moisture to freeze, even when the outside air temperature is above freezing. This can restrict airflow to the engine and reduce power. It is an engine hazard, not just an airframe icing hazard, and it can occur when there is no visible cloud.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Engine Handling - Carburettor Heat; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 19, Aircraft Icing

## MET-447
**Topic:** Meteorology - Icing Gaps  
**Type:** Multiple Choice  
**Difficulty:** Basic  
**Tags:** carburettor-icing, temperature, humidity

When can carburettor icing occur?

- A) In moist air, often at temperatures well above 0 C
- B) Only in visible cloud below 0 C
- C) Only during heavy snow
- D) Only when the aircraft is parked overnight

**Answer:** A - In moist air, often at temperatures well above 0 C.

**Explanation:** Carburettor icing is different from structural icing. The carburettor can become much colder than the outside air because of venturi cooling and fuel vaporization. If enough moisture is present, ice can form even on a warm-looking day. For PPL flying, this is why temperature and humidity matter, and why pilots are taught to be alert for carb ice during reduced power operation, descent, or damp conditions.

**Reference:** Transport Canada Flight Training Manual, Engine Handling - Carburettor Heat; FAA Aviation Weather Handbook, Ch. 19, Induction Icing

- A) A gradual drop in RPM
- B) A sudden increase in RPM
- C) A higher oil temperature with no other change
- D) An immediate transponder failure

**Answer:** A - A gradual drop in RPM.

The change may be subtle at first, which is why regular instrument scanning matters. If the pilot waits until the engine is running very roughly, the ice may already be significant.

What is the normal immediate action if carburettor icing is suspected in a carburetted engine?

- A) Apply full carburettor heat and leave it on until the ice is cleared
- B) Lean the mixture fully and wait
- C) Turn off the fuel selector
- D) Close the throttle and glide without using carb heat

**Answer:** A - Apply full carburettor heat and leave it on until the ice is cleared.

**Explanation:** Full carburettor heat directs warmed air into the carburettor to melt the ice. Partial heat may not provide enough warmth and can sometimes leave the carburettor in an icing temperature range. When heat is first applied, engine roughness may increase briefly as melted ice and water pass through the engine. Removing heat too early can leave ice behind.

- B) It increases lift on the wings
- C) It removes frost from the tailplane

**Answer:** A - Low power can reduce engine heat while carb ice continues to form.

- A) A decrease in engine power because the engine receives warmer, less dense air

- C) Elimination of all structural icing

**Answer:** A - A decrease in engine power because the engine receives warmer, less dense air.

Why is frost on the wings dangerous?

- A) It roughens the surface, disrupts airflow, reduces lift, and increases drag
- B) It improves boundary layer control
- C) It only matters on jet aircraft

**Answer:** A - It roughens the surface, disrupts airflow, reduces lift, and increases drag.

- C) Take off if the runway is long enough

## MET-474
**Topic:** Meteorology - Icing Gaps  
**Type:** Open Answer  
**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Tags:** icing-escape, checklist, vfr

List practical actions a VFR pilot should consider after detecting ice on a non-icing-equipped aircraft.

**Answer:**
Practical actions include:

- Leave visible moisture immediately
- Turn back toward known better weather if available
- Descend to warmer air if terrain, cloud clearance, and regulations allow
- Climb above the layer only if performance, oxygen, cloud clearance, and forecast tops make that clearly safe
- Use pitot heat and other anti-ice/de-ice equipment if installed and approved
- Avoid abrupt manoeuvres and avoid slow flight
- Inform ATC or FSS, request assistance if needed, and provide a PIREP
- Divert or land at the nearest suitable airport before the situation worsens

**Explanation:** Icing is a time-critical problem. The aircraft is becoming less efficient while the pilot may be losing visibility, performance, and instrument reliability. A good response is not one magic action; it is a disciplined escape plan. The pilot should use every available resource while keeping the aircraft under control and moving out of the icing environment.

Explain why a "thin" layer of frost can be more dangerous than it looks.

**Answer:**
A thin frost layer can be dangerous because the wing does not only need the right general shape; it also needs a reasonably smooth surface. Frost creates small roughness elements that disturb airflow and can make the boundary layer separate earlier.

This can cause:
- Reduced lift
- Increased drag
- Higher stall speed
- Longer takeoff roll
- Worse climb performance
- Less predictable control response

**Explanation:** The visual thickness of frost is misleading. A pilot may see only a light white coating and assume the aircraft will fly normally, but the aerodynamic penalty can be significant.
